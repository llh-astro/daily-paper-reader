Climates of temperate rocky planets with He-dominated atmospheres 

Viviane Kuss ,1, 2 Robin Wordsworth ,1, 3 Collin Cherubim ,1, 4 and Jessica Cmiel 1, 3

ABSTRACT We present radiative-convective modeling of rocky exoplanets with He-dominated atmospheres and low envelope mass fractions. Helium has a steeper adiabatic temperature profile than N 2 and H 2 as it has fewer degrees of freedom. Line broadening differences are small, collision-induced absorption (CIA) is not important in He-dominated atmospheres, and Rayleigh scattering by He is weak. The combined impact of these effects is that He-dominated atmospheres provide more warming than N 2-dominated atmospheres but less than H 2-dominated atmospheres, for the same surface pressure. For surface pressures in the range 1-20 bar, the habitable zone for rocky planets around M dwarfs with He-dominated atmospheres is narrower than for H 2-dominated atmospheres. Nonetheless, due to their large scale height compared to N 2 or CO 2-dominated atmospheres, temperate He-dominated atmospheres are favorable targets for characterization via transit spectroscopy with JWST.

1. INTRODUCTION Sub-Neptunes are a common class of planets, with more than half of Sun-like stars hosting one or more of them (Bean et al. 2021), although they are notably absent from our solar system. Hu et al. (2015) first proposed the existence of sub-Neptunes with He-dominated atmospheres as a result of differentiation during atmospheric escape in He/H 2-dominated envelopes. The resulting He-dominated atmospheres may be subject to further mass-loss processes, ultimately leading to planets that lose their primordial envelopes entirely. Zhang et al. (2023) reported the detection of He in the outflows of sub-Neptunes orbiting K dwarfs. Furthermore, a broad population of sub-Neptunes with He-dominated atmospheres at the upper edge of the radius valley was predicted by Cherubim et al. (2024), Malsky et al. (2023), Gu and Chen (2023) and Malsky and Rogers (2020). Coupled atmosphere-interior simulations predict many of these atmospheres will be oxidized relative to solar composition due to preferential H escape (Cherubim et al. 2025). Here, we present the results of the first radiative-convective climate modeling of He-dominated atmospheres. We study the physical effects that cause He to differ from other background gases and explore the possibility of observable He-dominated atmospheres of rocky exoplanets that originated as sub-Neptunes (Pierrehumbert and Gaidos 2011). We give a short overview of our methods and report our key results in Section 3. We discuss the implications regarding detectability and atmospheric evolution in Section 4, and explore the possibility of He-dominated ocean worlds. Conclusions are given in Section 5. 

2. METHOD We use the Planetary Climate Model Line-By-Line (PCM–LBL) one-dimensional radiative-convective model (Wordsworth et al. 2017, 2021; Cmiel et al. 2025) to model He-dominated atmospheres. PCM–LBL is an open-source code that solves the equations of radiative transfer in a plane-parallel atmosphere using line absorption data from the HITRAN database. The model iteratively evolves the temperature towards radiative-convective equilibrium by balancing absorbed shortwave radiation (ASR) and outgoing longwave radiation (OLR). At every timestep, when local super-adiabatic profiles, convective adjustment restores a moist adiabatic profile. Regions of the atmosphere that arXiv:2607.06857v1 [astro-ph.EP] 7 Jul 2026 2are stable to convection evolve to radiative equilibrium. Turbulent planetary boundary layer behavior is not taken into account separately from moist convection. Further details are given in Wordsworth et al. (2017) and Cmiel et al. (2025). To adapt PCM–LBL to He-dominated atmospheres, we incorporated new refractive indices for Rayleigh scattering as well as He line broadening and He-CO 2 collision-induced absorption (CIA) data (Pierrehumbert 2010; Tan et al. 2022). We performed simulations using both a G dwarf spectrum (solar) and an M dwarf spectrum (surface temperature Ts

= 3500 K, log g = 5.0, and m = 0.0 dex, provided in the Phoenix Library; Husser et al. 2013). The M dwarf spectrum resembles that of LHS 1140 and K2-18, though the temperature is chosen to be closer to K2-18 to maintain consistency across this work, including the transmission-spectrum modeling performed using PICASO . This choice is not critical at fixed received stellar instellation flux, as the resulting atmosphere profiles vary only slightly across stellar temperatures 

Ts from 3100 K to 3500 K and for variations in log g between 0.5 and 5.0; in all cases, Tsurf variations were less than 0.5 K. To elucidate the radiative-convective properties of He atmospheres, we first modeled a hypothetical 1 bar He-dominated atmosphere and compared it to H 2- and N 2-dominated atmospheres, respectively. In all three cases, our modeled atmospheres contain 1% CO 2, condensable H 2O with fixed tropospheric relative humidity of 0.80 and constant stratospheric molar concentration, and a received stellar instellation flux of 700 W m −2.We next ran the model while varying orbital distance, for the M dwarf case only. On Earth-like planets with surface liquid water, the CO 2 concentration may change with orbital distance due to the carbonate-silicate cycle or related feedbacks (Walker et al. 1981). To account for this possibility, we produced results for three different CO 2 molar con-centrations (1%, 50%, 95%). We also ran simulations for two different surface pressures ( ps = 1 bar and ps = 20 bar). We validated the model results at high temperature and CO 2 molar concentrations by comparing with Wordsworth and Pierrehumbert (2013). To explore the detectability of potentially habitable He-dominated atmospheres, we used the open-source code 

PICASO (Batalha et al. 2019), which simulates ideal transmission spectra for exoplanets. We used the planet LHS 1140 b as a reference point, implementing its planetary and stellar properties as described in Cadieux et al. (2024). LHS 1140 b is predicted by Cherubim et al. (2025) to have a 59% probability of having at least 30% atmospheric He-concentration, provided the planet formed in-situ as a sub-Neptune. Measurements of escaping helium from the planet are consistent with a He-dominated atmosphere (Cherubim et al., in review). It therefore serves as a useful test case for the exploration of possible He-dominated transit spectra. We implemented molar concentrations of 99% H2, N 2, and He, containing 1% CO 2 each, similar to the initial atmosphere profiles characterizing the properties of He atmospheres. In all cases, variable H 2O was included and the surface pressure was 20 bar. He-rich atmospheres with low H are expected to be depleted in CH 4 and rich in CO 2, as described in Hu et al. (2015) and Cherubim et al. (2025). Lastly, we simulated JWST data utilizing the open-source code PandExo (Batalha et al. (2017)) with the transmission spectra created by PICASO as input. PandExo generates the in-transit and out-of-transit models and subsequently utilizes Pandeia to apply the corresponding point spread function and account for background noise, ramp noise, read noise, as well as flat-field errors. In this case, we chose NIRSpec PRISM since it covers the desired wavelength range (including our target CO 2 and H 2O bands).

RESULTS The results for the 1 bar modeling given He, H 2 and N 2 dominated atmospheres are shown in Figure 1. For these atmospheres, an incoming stellar flux of 700 W m −2 is assumed, as well as 1% CO 2 molar concentration and condensable H2O with a relative humidity of 0.80. The altitude-temperature plot illustrates the large differences in scale height for the three atmospheres due to their differing mean molecular masses. The H 2-H 2 CIA effects are significant and lead to notably higher surface temperature in the H 2-dominated cases. In contrast, N 2-N 2 and He-CO 2 CIA have an almost negligible effect compared to water vapor at these temperatures. Line broadening differences among N 2, He and H 2 are also small. In the G dwarf case, Rayleigh scattering causes a lower surface temperature in the N 2 case (see top left plot in Figure 1), while the lower polarizability of H 2 and He leads to weak Rayleigh scattering and higher surface temperature. The effects of Rayleigh scattering are muted for all compositions in the M dwarf case due to the red-shifted spectrum (Kasting et al. 1993; Wordsworth et al. 2010). The most important difference between He and N 2 in these simulations comes from the adiabat slope in the convective lower region of the atmosphere (i.e., the troposphere). In the 1 bar scenario, the 95% cases of both H 2 and He converge, whereas in the 20 bar case the warming effect of the H 2 atmospheres due to CIA dominates. In the 20 bar scenario, the He-dominated atmosphere is much warmer than the 5% He, 95% CO 2 atmosphere at small orbital distances (more than 50 K difference in Tsurf at 0.2 au). The same is true for the H 2 atmospheres at the inner edge, but to a smaller extent. This may be due to the uniquely large scale height and water-vapor column of a H 2 atmosphere, which reduces OLR and increases surface temperature (Koll and Cronin 2019). Moreover, the He-dominated atmospheres lead to higher Tsurf at the inner edge compared to H 2-dominated atmo-spheres. This is due to the extremely low albedo of the He-dominated case compared to the H 2-dominated atmospheres at the inner edge (0.021 vs. 0.17 at 0.2 au, see Figure 2). The H 2-dominated atmospheres have the highest albedo due to stronger Rayleigh scattering per unit column compared to CO 2 and He. The increase in albedo at intermediate orbital distances in the 99% and 90% He cases is a consequence of variation of the water concentration. Absorption by water vapor dominates over scattering by the background gases at low orbital distances due to He’s weak Rayleigh scattering, but as surface temperature decreases, this effect becomes weaker allowing Rayleigh scattering due to He to dominate. Below 200 K, the absorption by water vapor is negligible. This was validated by modeling a 100% He atmosphere with condensable H 2O, which resulted in an almost transparent atmosphere below 200 K. This caused the planetary albedo to approach the surface albedo, and the surface temperature to be close to the value expected on an airless planet. The He-dominated atmospheres also enter a runaway greenhouse regime sooner at close orbital distances, due to their lower albedos. This result should be seen as an upper limit on the He-dominated runaway greenhouse transition, because our clear-sky calculations neglect the reflective effects of clouds and aerosols. If an atmosphere contains species that have a higher molecular weight than the main atmospheric component, they may enter the Guillot regime (Guillot 1995; Seeley and Wordsworth 2025). If the Guillot threshold is crossed, warmer air packages no longer sustain buoyancy due to their high relative weight relative to the background gas. As a result, convection is suppressed and heat transport is dominated by radiation, leading to the formation of a radiative, supera-diabatic layer (Habib and Pierrehumbert 2025). The threshold can be calculated according to eq. (13) in Seeley and Wordsworth (2025). For the He-dominated atmospheres (both 1 bar and 20 bar), the Guillot regime may affect our results for received stelalr flux values larger than around 750 W m −2, and for the 1 bar H 2-dominated atmospheres for flux values larger than around 460 W m −2. At 20 bar, the H 2-dominated atmospheres may exhibit suppressed convection at all orbital distances. The PCM Line-By-Line code does not explicitly handle convection in the Guillot regime, so this question is left to future work. 5In summary, He-dominated atmospheres have steeper tropospheric adiabats compared to atmospheres with di- or triatomic background gases. In the clear-sky case, they exhibit a narrower habitable zone compared to H 2-dominated atmospheres, with the inner edge shifting outward and the outer edge inward. At the inner edge, this shift is due to the lower albedo of He atmospheres. At the outer edge, the more rapid freezing occurs due to the lack of strong CIA absorption in key infrared spectral regions compared to H 2.

Despite the narrow range of received stellar fluxes and surface pressures over which He-dominated atmospheres permit surface liquid water, they are favorable targets for transit spectroscopy. This can be seen in Figure 3, which shows a transmission spectrum generated using PICASO for H 2, He and N 2 dominated atmospheres, alongside a JWST detectability analysis employing PandExo . Additionally, spectroscopy results for LHS 1140 b reported by Damiano et al. (2024) are overplotted onto the modeled spectra. Both Damiano et al. (2024) and Cadieux et al. (2024) argued against the possibility of hazes in LHS 1140 b’s atmosphere based on H 2-atmosphere modeling by Hu (2021). However, more modeling with He as the background gas is necessary to fully exclude the possibility of e.g. sulfur-based hazes as a potential cause of the discrepancy between observational data of LHS 1140 b’s atmosphere and the modeled spectra. Due to the much lower scale height in the N 2-dominated case [14.6 km (N 2) versus 106.5 km (H 2) and 70.5 km (He)], its spectrum is almost flat. In contrast, the spectra of the H 2 and He atmospheres have significantly deeper features and the 2.0 μm, 2.7 μm and 4.3 μm absorption lines of CO 2 are clearly recognizable in their reconstructed spectra. This indicates He-dominated atmospheres will be significantly easier to characterize with infrared spectroscopy than atmospheres dominated by N 2, or other heavy species such as CO 2.6

DISCUSSION We have focused on extremely low mass fraction He-dominated atmospheres here for modeling convenience and to elucidate the key physical features. Such atmospheres are possible as the result of fractionation during mass loss of a primordial H 2-He envelope (Hu et al. 2015; Malsky et al. 2023; Cherubim et al. 2024; Lammer et al. 2025). While they are likely to be transient, due to continued He loss to space over time, they can remain stable on Gyr timescales (Cherubim et al. 2025). These values likely constitute lower limits on the atmospheric erosion timescales, as they neglect interactions with the planetary interior, and radiative cooling by metal-rich species. For He-rich planets with Earth-like or larger H 2O and CO 2 inventories, the runaway greenhouse transition would likely lead to a substantial increase in mean molecular mass in the atmosphere, due to conversion of liquid water oceans and surface carbonate deposits to H 2O vapor and gaseous CO 2, respectively. This could conceivably lead to population-level differences in the transit spectra of rocky exoplanets in the He-enrichment part of the radius valley (Figure 4). It would be interesting to explore this possibility further in future work. It will also be interesting to extend our analysis to higher pressure cases in future work, as these are expected to be more common. Above 20 bar, the Guillot effect (Seeley and Wordsworth 2025) as well as the effects of supercritical interiors (Pierrehumbert 2023) will play an important role and be integral to an accurate analysis. Future studies should also investigate the effects of clouds and aerosols on both albedo and infrared radiation in He-dominated atmospheres.

CONCLUSIONS We presented radiative-convective and transmission spectrum modeling results for rocky exoplanets with He-dominated atmospheres using the open-source codes PCM-LBL, PICASO and PandExo . Our key conclusions are: 1. He atmospheres exhibit a steeper temperature gradient in the troposphere compared to atmospheres dominated by diatomic gases, which leads to a stronger greenhouse effect when CO 2 and H 2O are also present. 72. He exhibits lower Rayleigh scattering than diatomic gas species, but line broadening differences among He, H 2

and N 2 are small. 3. Liquid surface water is possible in He-dominated atmospheres, but for a narrower range of orbital distances than for H 2 atmospheres. 4. Cloud-free He-dominated atmospheres containing CO 2, H 2O or similar infrared absorbers on rocky M dwarf planets like LHS 1140 b are detectable by JWST.
