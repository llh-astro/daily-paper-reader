ABSTRACT Intense high-energy irradiation of close-in gaseous exoplanets drives the rapid escape of their atmo-spheres, fundamentally shaping planetary demographics. While atmospheric loss is routinely observed via transit absorption in atomic hydrogen, helium, and metal ions, the underlying physical properties, specifically the thermal structure, outflow dynamics, and mass-loss rate, remain poorly constrained due to inherent degeneracies in the transmission geometry. Here we report the first detection of atomic hydrogen emission from the escaping atmosphere of a gas giant. Using high-resolution spectroscopy of the ultrahot Jupiter KELT-9 b, we detect a hydrogen Balmer line (H α 6564.6 ˚ A) emission signature originating from the planetary dayside. The emission line profile features a distinctive double-peaked shape with 0 .1–0 .15% peak amplitudes at ±30 km s −1 and central self-absorption. This profile breaks transmission degeneracies, providing direct observational constraints on the vertical thermal structure, excited-state hydrogen populations, and wind dynamics in the upper atmosphere of KELT-9 b. Initial modeling reveals a vigorous outflow with a mass-loss rate above 10 13 g s −1, among the highest mea-sured to date for gaseous exoplanets. Our results establish hydrogen airglow emission as a powerful diagnostic of atmospheric escape, opening a new observational window into the evolution of worlds in extreme radiation environments.

This shift in mech-anism stems from the distinct spectral energy distribu-tions of early-type host stars, which exhibit relatively weak EUV but intense near-ultraviolet (NUV; λ < 4000 ˚A) emission. Consequently, NUV radiation becomes the primary energy source deposited into the UHJ up-per atmosphere and drives the escape (Garc´ ıa Mu˜ noz & Schneider 2019). This process would lead to higher mass-loss rates than EUV-driven models, significantly impacting the long-term evolution of these atmospheres (Garc´ ıa Mu˜ noz & Schneider 2019; Fossati et al. 2020; Wyttenbach et al. 2020; Yan et al. 2021; Huang et al. 2023). Transmission spectra, though rich in atomic tracers, suffer from an inherent degeneracy between thermo-spheric temperature, gas density, and mass-loss rate (Wyttenbach et al. 2020; Linssen et al. 2022). Emission from the escaping gas could break this degeneracy by probing the vertical temperature and density structure directly, but such emission has been predicted to be faint and previous searches in other hot Jupiters were incon-clusive (Menager et al. 2013; Rosener et al. 2025; Zhang et al. 2020). Here we present the detection of Hα (6564.6 ˚A) emission from the dayside of KELT-9 b using the high-resolution optical spectrograph Keck Planet Finder (KPF; Gibson et al. 2020, 2024) on the Keck I tele-scope. Such emission is the exoplanet analogue of air-glow, the faint optical glow produced in Earth’s upper atmosphere as atoms relax from excited states popu-lated by solar radiation. Our observations spectrally resolved the line into a distinctive double-peaked pro-file with central self-absorption, which records the ther-mal structure, the excited-state hydrogen population, and the wind dynamics of the escaping atmosphere. We interpret this profile with a one-dimensional non-local-thermodynamic-equilibrium (NLTE) outflow model and use it to place the first emission-based constraint on the escaping atmosphere of a hot Jupiter.

OBSERVATIONS AND DATA REDUCTION We observed emission from KELT-9 b across multiple orbital phases using KPF on UT 2024 August 8, 20, and 27 (PI: Y. Zhang). KPF is a high-resolution echelle spec-trometer mounted on the Keck I telescope covering the optical wavelength range 445–870 nm with a resolving power of R ∼ 98 000. Given the planet’s large orbital semi-amplitude ( Kp ∼ 245 km s −1), we used an expo-sure time of 150 s to minimize phase smearing of the planetary signal. Observing conditions were favourable, with airmass between 1.07 and 1.70, and average seeing ranging from 0.3 ′′ on August 8 to 0.5 ′′ on August 20 and 27 in V -band. This yielded a typical signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) per pixel of ∼400, 330, and 320 at 650 nm for the three nights, respectively. Total on-target inte-gration times were 3.5 h (85 exposures), 2.5 h (59 ex-posures), and 5.2 h (125 exposures). The observations span a range of orbital phases: ϕ = 0 .30–0 .42 (Night 1), 

ϕ = 0 .40–0 .48 (Night 2), and ϕ = 0 .05–0 .25 (Night 3); see Fig. A1. We used spectra processed by the KPF Data Re-duction Pipeline (Gibson et al. 2020), which performs standard flat-fielding, order tracing, and optimal extrac-tion. We further performed blaze correction using the blaze functions extracted from the smooth lamp pattern (Householder et al. 2025). Wavelength calibration was achieved using laser-frequency-comb and thorium-argon exposures bracketing the science observations. Telluric absorption features from H 2O and O 2 were corrected using the ESO sky tool Molecfit (Smette et al. 2015). Figure A1 demonstrates the telluric removal for the two spectral orders covering 652–662 nm. The telluric-corrected spectra were shifted to the stellar rest frame by accounting for the systemic and barycentric velocities and the star’s reflex motion. Amaster stellar spectrum was generated for each night by average-combining the respective time series. Individual exposures were divided by the nightly master spectrum to remove stationary stellar features. Observational un-certainties were estimated from the standard deviation of the residuals in each wavelength channel. We focus on the two spectral orders that each indepen-dently cover the H α line. Around the stellar H α line cen-tre, we identified a prominent residual pattern caused by stellar pulsation, as previously reported in photometric and spectroscopic studies (Wong et al. 2020; Wytten-bach et al. 2020). This pulsation signal contaminates the data within the stellar rotational velocity profile (v sin i ∼ 110 km s −1). While the planetary radial ve-locity exceeds this threshold at most orbital phases, the stellar contamination overlaps with the planetary track at ϕ > 0.4. We therefore masked the data within the affected velocity range ( |v| < 110 km s −1 in the stellar rest frame) to guarantee an unbiased extraction of the planetary signature.

DETECTION OF H α EMISSION As shown in Fig. A1, the two-dimensional time-series residuals after telluric and stellar removal show a pair of bright, curved traces that shift with the expected Kep-lerian motion of the planet. This Doppler signature un-ambiguously identifies the excess emission as planetary in origin. Because the wavelength region around H α is simultaneously covered by two independent spectral or-Hydrogen airglow 3ders of KPF, we can confidently rule out instrumental artefacts as we detect consistent features in both orders. Shifting the spectra into the planet’s rest frame using a semi-amplitude of Kp = 244 .8 km s −1 (Zhang et al. 2026), and averaging across phases where the dayside dominates the visible hemisphere ( ϕ = 0 .30–0 .45), we detect the integrated H α emission at 10 σ significance, with an equivalent width of 0 .00080 ± 0.00008 ˚ A, as shown in Fig. 1a. The combined line profile is intrigu-ingly non-Gaussian: it shows two emission peaks at ±30 km s −1, with the line core absorbed down to the con-tinuum. The peaks are asymmetric, with a stronger red wing (amplitude ∼ 0.15%) and a weaker blue wing (∼ 0.10%). No significant signal is recovered at phases where most of the dayside is hidden ( ϕ = 0 .10–0 .25, Fig. A2), as expected if the emission originates from the irradiated hemisphere. Subdividing the detection into finer phase bins reveals the phase-dependence of the emission signal. The emis-sion feature shifts progressively redward as the orbital phase advances towards secondary eclipse (Fig. 1b&c). The extent of the shift is ∼3.8 km s −1 from ϕ ∼ 0.3 to 0.45 in the 2D model. The same trend has been observed in dayside metal lines of KELT-9 b, which is attributed to the planet rotation and horizontal winds (Zhang et al. 2026); see also Section 5. 

4. NLTE OUTFLOW MODEL 4.1. Outflow structure 

To interpret the double-peaked morphology, we devel-oped a one-dimensional NLTE outflow model of KELT-9 b. The model uses a customized version of the 

sunbather code (Linssen et al. 2024), with an iterative coupling between the radiative energy balance solved by the NLTE Cloudy code (c23.01, Gunasekera et al. 2023) and the mass and momentum continuity equations. The original sunbather adopts an isothermal Parker wind density and velocity profile assuming a single tem-perature Tiso and mass-loss rate ˙M , generated via the 

p-winds package (Dos Santos et al. 2022). Cloudy 

then solves the energy balance and computes a non-isothermal temperature and ionization structure, ac-counting for radiative heating and cooling from atomic species given the input stellar irradiation. However, in the default sunbather framework, the underlying isothermal Parker wind structure is not self-consistent with the non-isothermal temperature profile returned by 

Cloudy . Because H α emission is sensitive to both ther-mal structure and density profile, this inconsistency can bias the predicted line shape. We adopt a PHOENIX model with Teff = 10 000 K as the input stellar spectrum (Husser et al. 2013), since A-type stars are expected to have little extreme-ultraviolet (XUV) emission due to the lack of chromosphere/corona (Fossati et al. 2018), and KELT-9 only has an reported upper limit on the XUV luminosity (Sanz-Forcada et al. 2025). This NUV energy budget is incorporated in Cloudy to compute the outflow structure self-consistently. 

Cloudy calculations become unreliable at number den-sities above 10 15 cm −3 (Fossati et al. 2021), which is the case in the lower atmosphere of KELT-9 b. We therefore mask the high-pressure levels ( P > 10 −3 bar) in high-˙M cases, and extrapolate the resulting temperature pro-file to the masked lower atmosphere based on literature studies of the hydrostatic atmosphere, which suggest a dayside photospheric temperature of ∼4,500 K and a steep temperature gradient (Zhang et al. 2026; Fossati et al. 2021). A model with a mass-loss rate ˙M = 10 13 g s −1 or higher provides a broadly good match to the observed line profile (Fig. A3), reproducing both the amplitude and the double-peaked morphology. The correspond-ing atmospheric profiles (Fig. 2) show a temperature inversion rising from ∼4,500 K at the photosphere to 

∼8,000 K near 1.9 Rp, followed by expansion cooling of the escaping gas, and an NLTE departure of the excited-state hydrogen populations above ∼1.3 Rp. Together these features produce a unique vertical structure in the Hα source function, which in turn drives the observed line shape through the following physical effects (Fig. 3): 

• Temperature inversion drives emission. If Ly-man α photons produced by thermal excitation in the lower atmosphere cannot escape due to colli-sional de-excitation after extensive scattering, the excited-state number density ratio, n2p/n 1s, ap-proximately follows LTE conditions. This also applies to the n3/n 2 ratio. Consequently, the 

n3/n 2 ratio rises with temperature, boosting the Hα source function and producing net emission in the line wings, which are formed at these depths. 

• NLTE effects suppress the source function. Above 

∼1.3 Rp, the n = 3 level population transitions from collision-dominated to radiation-dominated regimes as the number density becomes sufficiently low such that collisional excitation rates from n =2 to n = 3 fall below the radiative excitation rates driven by stellar H α. The resulting NLTE depar-ture drives n3/n 2 downwards (Fig. 2), while adi-abatic expansion of the outflow further cools the gas. These effects create an absorbing layer above the emitting region. 

• Self-absorption carves the line core. Because H α

The H α emission shows a similar progressive red-shift with phase (Fig. 1b&c). This shift arises as the planet rotates, an increasing fraction of the dayside hemisphere rotates into view, and the day-to-night winds advect atmospheric gas away from the observer (Fig. 3). The spectrally- and phase-resolved H α emission thus reflects a consis-tent picture of global atmospheric circulation with the metal lines. Our 1D model reproduces the overall morphology and its phase variation, but the observed line is broader than the model prediction, hinting at underestimated thermal broadening, scattering of stellar H α radiation, or three-dimensional wind patterns that our one-dimensional treatment cannot capture. The plane-parallel radiative transfer also becomes less valid in the extended layers probed by the line core. These effects do not alter the double-peaked morphology, which is set by the vertical NLTE structure, but they do influence the detailed line Hydrogen airglow 7shape. Future three-dimensional hydrodynamic simula-tions and NLTE radiative transfer calculations will be needed to fully exploit the constraining power of H α

emission. S´ anchez-L´ opez et al. (2022) reported the transmission detection of Paschen β line with a contrast of ∼0.53%, a blueshift of ∼14.8 km s −1. This line probes the same ex-cited state n = 3 as the H α emission line. S´ anchez-L´ opez et al. (2022) interpreted the blueshift as the day-to-night flow in the bound atmosphere ( ∼1.3 Rp), which is con-sistent with our interpretation of the phase-dependent Doppler shifts observed in the emission line. Alterna-tively, their study proposed that the origin of Pa β ab-sorption could be in a tail of escaping gas moving toward the observer. Although less probable, this scenario could potentially be viable if flaring events arising from star-planet interactions populate the H( n = 3) in the escap-ing tail (S´ anchez-L´ opez et al. 2022). Future modeling work combining all available observations of the absorp-tion lines (Balmer and Paschen series) and the H-alpha emission will provide tight constraints on the vertical structure of the H population.

IMPLICATIONS FOR MASS LOSS The emission profile lifts the temperature–mass-loss-rate degeneracy that persists in transmission-based studies of atmospheric escape. Previous H α transmis-sion analyses in KELT-9 b inferred ˙M ∼ 10 12 –10 12 .8

g s −1 (Yan & Henning 2018; Wyttenbach et al. 2020); the recent Mg ii /Fe ii near-ultraviolet (NUV) measure-ment gave ˙M ∼ 10 12 –10 12 .5 g s −1 (Baldwin et al. 2025); and self-consistent models predict up to ∼ 10 12 .5–10 13 

g s −1 for a 2 MJup to 1.2 MJup planet (Garc´ ıa Mu˜ noz & Schneider 2019). The planet mass itself is poorly known because of the star’s rapid rotation, with literature val-ues ranging from 2 .17 ± 0.56 to 2 .88 ± 0.35 MJup (Gaudi et al. 2017; Borsa et al. 2019; Hoeijmakers et al. 2019; Pai Asnodkar et al. 2022), compounding the uncertainty. Our emission-based analysis requires ˙M ∼ 10 13 g s −1

or higher to produce the double-peaked line morphology (Fig. A3): higher mass-loss rates (and the accompanying higher densities and pressures) push the LTE-to-NLTE transition outward, which is essential to produce the unique source-function profile in Fig. 2. Mass-loss rates much above 10 13 g s −1 would require an unphysically high base pressure ( P > 10 bar) at 1 Rp, inconsistent with the expected photospheric level. Our value there-fore sits at the upper end of the literature range and favours a planet at the low end of the reported mass in-terval. This mass-loss rate of 10 13 g s −1 means that the planet could lose ∼8% of its total mass in 1 Gyr. This is at the extreme high end of reported atmo-spheric escape rates for the broader population of tran-siting planets, such as warm Neptunes, hot Jupiters, and other ultrahot Jupiters ( ˙M ∼ 10 8–10 12 g s −1) (Ehrenre-ich et al. 2015; Bourrier et al. 2018; des Etangs et al. 2010; Zhang et al. 2023; Czesla et al. 2024; Allart et al. 2025). Notably, the high mass-loss rates tend to be found in planets with high Roche filling factors (Rp/R Hill ) orbiting early-type stars, which facilitate at-mospheric escapes (Saidel et al. 2025). Compared to typical ultrahot Jupiters orbiting F-type stars, KELT-9 b has a relatively lower Roche filling factor of ∼0.39 (versus ∼0.5 for WASP-121 b) and weaker X-ray and ex-treme ultraviolet (XUV) irradiation from its A-type host star. Therefore, the higher mass-loss rate in KELT-9 b supports the alternative Balmer-driven escape mecha-nism that the stellar NUV irradiation can power the outflow in planets orbiting A-type stars (Garc´ ıa Mu˜ noz & Schneider 2019). Future emission models accounting for 3D effects will allow for improved constraints on the mass-loss rate.

CONCLUSION We present the detection of H α (6564.6 ˚ A) emission line from the dayside of the ultra-hot Jupiter KELT-9 b using the high-resolution spectrometer KPF on the Keck I telescope. This represents the first hydrogen emission detected in any escaping atmosphere. Intriguingly, the spectrally resolved H α emission shows double peaks with amplitudes of 0 .1 − 0.15% at ±30 km s −1 along with an absorption feature in the line center. We apply NLTE outflow modeling using customized sunbather code and compute synthetic spectra to interpret the observed line profile. This double-peaked line profile is a consequence of the extreme atmospheric condition, which leads to an in-crease of the excited-state H i n3/n 2 ratio due to strong thermal inversion in the lower atmosphere and a fall-off due to NLTE effect in the upper atmosphere. This causes the self-absorption of the H α emission in the upper atmosphere, resulting in the double-peaked line shape. We find that a large mass-loss rate of ˙M ∼ 10 13 

g s −1 is required to produce the line profile. It also pro-vides key dynamical information on the escaping atmo-sphere: the asymmetric double peaks indicate a vertical outflow wind speed of ∼5 km s −1 at the altitude probed by the H α line core; the phase-dependent Doppler shift points to a horizontal day-to-night winds of ∼12 km s −1.The detection of spectrally- and phase-resolved hy-drogen airglow from KELT-9 b demonstrates that the thermal emission is a powerful tracer that can probe the atmospheric escape of short-period exoplanets. The 8 Zhang et al. 

double-peaked line morphology directly records the ver-tical thermal structure, the NLTE departure of the excited-state populations, the outflow wind speed, and the day-to-night circulation—quantities that transmis-sion alone cannot separate. Extending this diagnostic to other hot Jupiters, and to other transitions accessi-ble from the ground, will open a new observational win-dow on the escape and evolution of worlds in extreme radiation environments.
