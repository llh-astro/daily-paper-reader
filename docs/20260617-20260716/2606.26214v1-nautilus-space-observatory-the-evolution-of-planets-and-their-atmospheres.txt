ABSTRACT We are just beginning to explore the billion-year evolution from nascent planets in disks to mature planetary systems. Recent discoveries hint at demographic and atmospheric differences between young planets and their Gyr-old counterparts, but current facilities are limited − particularly in their ability to conduct statistical atmospheric studies over a broad period range. This white paper outlines compelling science achievable with the Nautilus Space Observatory, a proposed constellation of large-diameter space telescopes. We identify four primary scientific objectives: (1) determining the timescales over which planets evolve into sub-Neptunes and super-Earths; (2) tracking the temporal evolution of atmospheric mass-loss rates; (3) characterizing the evolution of the atmospheric mean molecular weight and C/O ratio; and (4) identifying the emergence of Helium-dominated worlds. Answering these questions requires the high spatial resolution, broad-wavelength coverage, large effective area, and parallelized multiple units that Nautilus provides. By isolating the physical processes that govern the evolution of planets and their atmospheres, these science objectives directly support NASA’s Cosmic Origins and Exoplanet Exploration Programs. 

1. SCIENTIFIC CONTEXT AND PROBLEM STATEMENT This White Paper presents a potential science case for the Nautilus Space Observatory, a concept under development for a NASA Strategic Mission for the Astro 2030 Decadal Survey. Nautilus is a constellation of space telescopes and will provide a modular, scalable, sustainable, upgradable, expandable space observatory that can be deployed rapidly and then expanded progressively. The core concept for Nautilus is described in D. Apai et al. (2019). This White Paper is part of the first series of science white papers capturing ideas that emerged from the Nautilus Science Case workshop (held at MIT in May 2026). This White Paper focuses on the evolution of planets and their atmospheres. JWST and ALMA are providing unprecedented detail on the gas and dust of hundreds of protoplanetary disks during the first few tens of millions of years of planetary assembly (e.g., I. Pascucci et al. 2016; S. M. Andrews et al. 2018; F. Long et al. 2018; K. Zhang et al. 2025). In parallel, exoplanet surveys, relying primarily on the transit and radial velocity methods, have discovered over 6,000 exoplanets (e.g., L. J. Rosenthal et al. 2021; M. Kunimoto et al. 2022; J. J. Lissauer et al. 2024). However, the vast majority of these are Gyr-old planets and planetary systems. A billion years of evolution from nascent planets to mature systems is only now beginning to be explored.

2Two dozen young ( ∼ 20 −1, 000 Myr) Neptunes and sub-Neptunes recently discovered by K2 and TESS are providing the first evidence of demographic changes between nascent and mature systems. It is found that their frequency, within 10 days, is higher than that of their Gyr-old counterparts (J. L. Christiansen et al. 2023; S. Vach et al. 2024). This decline does not appear to be linear over time (R. B. Fernandes et al. 2025), which has been attributed to a combination of tidal migration and photoevaporation, though small-number statistics preclude firmly establishing this trend. In addition, the fraction of neighboring planets near a first-order orbital resonance is larger for the dozen of known younger than older systems (F. Dai et al. 2024). Finally, there is emerging evidence for atmospheric evolution. Recent HST and JWST spectroscopic observations of a few young planets indicate that they have clearer atmospheres and lower metallicities ( ∼solar to a few times solar), hence lower mean molecular weight, than older planets of comparable mass (P. C. Thao et al. 2024; S. Barat et al. 2025). Isolating the physical processes that drive the evolution of planets and their atmospheres requires detecting and characterizing a larger sample of young planets across a broader range of orbital periods. Current facilities cannot achieve this: TESS searches are restricted to nearby star-forming regions ( ≤ 200 pc) due to sensitivity and severe flux contamination from its large pixels (e.g., R. B. Fernandes et al. 2022); JWST characterization is limited by the sample of discovered young planets, mostly within 10 days, and high proposal oversubscription; and any Roman’s spectroscopic characterization is restricted to a narrow grism window (1.0–1.9 μm). Nautilus uniquely overcomes these bottlenecks. By combining high spatial resolution and broad-wavelength spectroscopy, with multiple units for efficient mapping of star-forming regions, Nautilus is the ideal facility to conduct comprehensive demographic studies of young planets and their atmospheres.

SCIENCE OBJECTIVES The demographics of young exoplanets, combined with the characterization of their atmospheres through transmis-sion spectroscopy, will enable Nautilus to answer the following questions (see Fig. 1 for an overview): 1. Over what timescale do planets evolve into sub-Neptunes and super-Earths? Sub-Neptunes ( ∼

1.8 − 4 R ⊕) and super-Earths ( ∼ 1.2 − 1.8 R ⊕) are the most common transiting exoplanets (e.g., J. J. Lissauer et al. 2023), yet we have no equivalent in the Solar System. Understanding which planets enter these classes, and when, provides crucial context for the origins of planetary systems. Answering this question will require the measurement of occurrence rates of planets around young ( ∼ 50 − 500 Myr) stars, with radii down to at least 

∼ 1.5 R ⊕ and periods extending to at least 50 days. The largest period is set by the transition period beyond which sub-Neptunes become more frequent than super-Earths around Gyr-old stars (G. J. Bergsten et al. 2022). 2. How does mass loss rate evolve in time? Atmospheric evaporation is one of the key processes invoked to explain the division between Gyr-old, mostly rocky super-Earths and larger, gas-rich or water-world sub-Neptunes (B. J. Fulton et al. 2017). Pinning down the evolution of the mass-loss rate will tell us whether most evaporation is driven by high-energy stellar photons early on or by core-powered mass loss at later times (e.g., J. E. Owen & Y. Wu 2017; S. Ginzburg et al. 2018). It will also help clarify whether other mechanisms, e.g., formation at different locations in the disk (e.g., R. Burn et al. 2024), are also critical in setting the separation of these two classes of planets. The He I line at 1.083 μm is a reliable tracer of atmospheric escape (e.g., M. Gully-Santiago et al. 2024). Even when not spectrally resolved, it can provide a first-order assessment of the mass-loss rate, especially when coupled with models that compute the wind thermal structure (D. C. Linssen et al. 2022). When spectrally resolved (requiring R ≥ 30 , 000) and combined with other hydrogen lines, it provides more stringent constraints on the mass-loss rate (e.g., M. Lamp´ on et al. 2021). 3. How do a planet’s atmospheric mean molecular weight and C/O ratio evolve with time? Billions of years of magma ocean outgassing and atmospheric escape severely contaminate the atmospheric compositions of Gyr-old planets (e.g., E. S. Kite & M. N. Barnett 2020; H. Nicholls et al. 2024). This erases signatures of their initial formation location in the disk and, for sub-Neptunes, their bulk composition (whether they are gas dwarfs of water worlds). Measuring the C/O ratio and mean molecular weight of young planets could reveal the formation locations and the nature of sub-Neptunes (e.g., N. Madhusudhan et al. 2016; J. G. Rogers 2025). For this science goal, the minimum requirement is to measure water abundances (even at moderate resolution 

R ∼ 100) to infer the scale height and mean molecular weight (e.g. B. Benneke & S. Seager 2013), while detecting CH 4 and CO 2 is necessary to estimate the C/O ratio (e.g., N. E. Batalha & M. R. Line 2017). 34. When do He-dominated planets emerge? Helium worlds are a robust prediction of atmospheric escape models, but they represent a transient state that lasts on the order of a hundred million years (e.g., R. Hu et al. 2015; C. Cherubim et al. 2025). They are expected to be located below the Neptune desert ( ∼ 3−10 R ⊕, < 4 days) and at the upper edge of the radius valley (C. Cherubim et al. 2024). Thus, their detection is important for understanding hydrodynamic atmospheric escape, as well as the origin of the Neptune desert and radius valley. These planets may be identified through the detection of He and CO 2 (e.g., K. Barkaoui et al. 2024), which are expected to be abundant, alongside constraints on CH 4, which is expected to be significantly depleted (e.g., C. Cherubim et al. 2025). DATA REQUIREMENTS Of the science objectives outlined above, only the first can be achieved with broad-band photometry while the others require transmission spectroscopy. 

• Photometry: Multiple filters ( ∼ 3) are required for photometric transit searches of young exoplanets and for validating detections. A 2-10 minute cadence is sufficient for this science case (but note that with 20-30 second cadence science questions related to stellar flares can be also addressed). To detect planets out to 50 days we require continuous monitoring for at least 150 days. A photometric precision of 15 ppm is needed to detect a super-Earth (=1.5 R ⊕) with SNR of 10 over 3.5 h (half of the transit duration for a planet at 50 days orbiting a Sun-like star). An angular resolution of ∼ 0.1′′ will expand the sample of accessible young stars by more than an order of magnitude and enable typical binary systems ( ∼ 40 au) to be resolved out to 400 pc. 

• Spectroscopy . The second science question requires modest spectroscopic capabilities covering from ∼ 0.4 −

1.5 μm at (R ∼ 500). High-precision optical and near-infrared spectra are needed to derive joint constraints on cloud/haze properties and chemical abundance (e.g., L. Welbanks & N. Madhusudhan 2019) and detect the He I line at 1.083 μm. Resolving the line (typical width ∼ 20 km/s) will require higher spectral resolution (R ≥ 30 , 000). Science objectives 3 and 4 can be achieved at lower spectral resolution ( R ∼ 1, 000). Water lines are present even in the short wavelength range mentioned above and are sufficient to address the evolution of a planet’s mean molecular weight. Detecting CO 2 and CH 4 (relevant for the C/O ratio and the identification of He worlds) requires extending the wavelength coverage to at least 2.5 μm.

ANALYSIS AND INTERPRETATION The necessary datasets are time-averaged photometry for Q1 and time-averaged spectra for Q2–Q4. Well estab-lished transit analysis scripts − originally developed for Kepler, TESS, and JWST − can be utilized to analyze both datasets. Despite the large photometric variability of young stars ( ∼ 10 , 000 ppm), modern detrending techniques have successfully pushed detection limits down to the super-Earth regime (e.g., A. W. Mann et al. 2018; M. G. Barber et al. 2025; R. B. Fernandes et al. 2025). 4

6. RELEVANCE TO NAUTILUS AND MISSION CLASS As mentioned in Sect. 1, current facilities face significant bottlenecks in characterizing a large sample of young planets across a broad range of orbital periods—limitations that Nautilus can overcome. Here, we further stress that because star-forming regions and associations are distributed across vast sky areas − often spanning tens of square degrees − a constellation of telescopes with the parallelized observing capabilities of Nautilus becomes essential for accelerating survey execution. 

7. RELEVANCE TO NASA AND ASTROPHYSICS STRATEGY Characterizing young exoplanetary systems directly supports NASA’s Cosmic Origins key question “How did we get here?” (Strategy 1.1 of NASA’s Science Plan 13 ). The physical evolution of smaller planets like super-Earths can also impact their habitability, inherently linking this research to a question central to NASA’s Exoplanet Exploration, “Are we alone?”. As such, the study of young exoplanets acts as a critical bridge between Cosmic Origins and Exoplanet Exploration, two NASA astrophysics science themes, and directly advances the Astro2020 Decadal Survey’s priority to understand “Worlds and Suns in Context” ( National Academies of Sciences & E. Medicine 2021). ACKNOWLEDGMENTS We thank the Heising-Simons Foundation for supporting the Nautilus Science Case Workshop.
