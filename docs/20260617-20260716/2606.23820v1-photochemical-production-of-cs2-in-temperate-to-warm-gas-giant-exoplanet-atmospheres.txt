ABSTRACT Sulfur chemistry has emerged as an important probe of exoplanet atmospheres in the JWST era, although observational constraints have thus far been largely limited to SO 2 and H 2S in warm and hot exoplanets. Recent JWST observations have revealed CS 2 in several cooler gas-giant exoplanets, yielding a new tracer of sulfur chemistry. However, the detailed chemical pathways responsible for the formation of CS 2 remain poorly understood. Here, we use TOI-6894 b, a temperate gas giant with evidence for CS 2, as a test case for one-dimensional photochemical kinetic-transport modeling and sensitivity analyses of CS 2 chemistry. We show that CS 2 is produced through coupled thermochemical and photochemical processes involving CH 4 and H 2S as the primary carbon and sulfur reservoirs, with S2 photolysis driving disequilibrium sulfur chemistry. Our models provide a self-consistent explanation for the observed CS 2 feature in TOI-6894 b. Extending our analysis to gas giant exoplanets spanning a wide range of Teq , we find that CS 2 abundance peaks in temperate to warm atmospheres ( Teq ∼ 500 – 700 K), and declines toward both lower and higher temperatures. This temperature dependence pro-vides a unified framework for interpreting current CS 2 observations, accounting for reported detections in temperate to warm planets and the lack of detections in colder and hotter giant exoplanets. Our results establish CS 2 as a complementary probe of sulfur inventories and atmospheric metallicity in cool gas giant exoplanets.

In this Letter, we investigate the photochemical pro-duction of CS 2 in giant exoplanet atmospheres. We perform forward modeling of temperate-to-warm giant exoplanet atmospheres, including calculations of atmo-spheric thermal structures and atmospheric chemical abundance profiles. Using TOI-6894 b as a case study as well as a broader sample of giant exoplanets span-ning a wide range of Teq , we explore the conditions under which CS 2 forms, identify its dominant chemi-cal pathways, and demonstrate that CS 2 represents an important sulfur-bearing species in disequilibrium atmo-spheric chemistry.

METHODS 2.1. Atmospheric T -P Profile Modeling ( PICASO )

We used PICASO 4.0 (J. Mang et al. 2026) to per-form one-dimensional radiative–convective equilibrium (RCE) calculations and generate self-consistent, planet-wide average T –P profiles for TOI-6894 b assuming full day–night heat redistribution 12 . The models span 91 pressure levels ranging from 10 −6 to 10 3 bar. We com-puted model grids spanning five atmospheric metallici-ties (0.1, 1, 3, 10, and 30 ×Z⊙, where Z⊙ denotes the solar elemental composition with C/O = 0.55 adopted from K. Lodders 2020) and five internal temperatures (Tint = 30, 60, 100, 200, and 400 K). The incident flux upon TOI-6894 b was calculated through interpo-lating the PHOENIX (F. Allard et al. 2012) grid of mod-els according to the host star’s stellar parameters, as described in S. Mukherjee et al. (2023). We note that the RCE and photochemical calculations (described in Section 2.3) were not self-consistently coupled. Previ-ous work has shown that disequilibrium chemistry has only a modest effect on the thermal structure of giant planet atmospheres with metallicities below 10 × Z⊙ (S. Mukherjee et al. 2025); therefore, we do not expect this approximation to significantly affect our results. 2.2. Chemical Networks 

The photochemical network was adopted from J. Yang & R. Hu (2024a), where it was constructed using the Reaction Mechanism Generator ( RMG ; C. W. Gao et al. 2016; M. Liu et al. 2021; M. S. Johnson et al. 2022), a rate-based automated chemical network generation framework widely used in chemical engineering (M. Liu et al. 2020; X. Dong et al. 2023). Built upon a contin-uously maintained database of experimental measure-

Using RMG enables the construction of chemically comprehensive and self-consistent reaction mechanisms while minimizing the risk of omitting important species and reactions. At the same time, species and reactions that contribute negligibly to a system of interest are ex-cluded, reducing computational expense without sacri-ficing chemical fidelity. RMG ’s comprehensive and systematic treatment of the reaction system (J. Yang et al. 2026). A current limitation of RMG , however, is that pho-tochemistry is not considered during network genera-tion. Consequently, species that are produced primarily through photochemistry normally be omitted from the resulting mechanism despite their important roles in at-mospheric photochemistry. This is the case for CS 2,which is generally not expected to be abundant under thermochemical equilibrium. To account for this limitation and incorporate CS 2

chemistry, we augmented the RMG -generated network with all reduced sulfur-bearing species and their as-sociated reactions that were absent from the RMG 

database but available in the VULCAN chemical net-work (S.-M. Tsai et al. 2017, 2021), specifically 

SNCHO photo network 2025.txt (S.-M. Tsai 2025). This included the species CS, CS 2, H 2CS, HCS, and NS, together with all associated photochemical and thermo-chemical reactions available in that network (3 photo-chemical and 36 thermochemical reactions). Thus, only a small fraction of the full SNCHO photo network 2025 

network was incorporated, while the underlying reaction mechanism remained the RMG -generated network. The resulting chemical network contains 103 species, includ-ing 33 sulfur-bearing species, and 2067 reactions. We additionally tested two alternative chemical net-works: the full VULCAN SNCHO photo network 2025 (S.-M. Tsai 2025), consisting of 91 species (21 sulfur-bearing species) and 593 reactions, and the Photochem network (N. F. Wogan et al. 2025), containing 92 species (20 sulfur-bearing species) and 682 reactions. Although not shown in this Letter, only the Photochem network failed to produce sufficient CS 2 within the JWST-observable region ( P ∼ 0.1−2 mbar; Z. Rustamkulov et al. 2023). This difference is briefly discussed further in Section 3.2. 2.3. One-dimensional Photochemical Kinetic-Transport Atmospheric Modeling ( EPACRIS )

We modeled the atmosphere of TOI-6894 b using the one-dimensional photochemical kinetic-transport code 

EPACRIS (J. Yang & R. Hu 2024b). For the stellar irra-diation input, we adopted the spectrum of the M5-type star GJ 876 from the MUSCLES survey III ( Teff = 3062 K; R. O. P. Loyd et al. 2016), whose effective temperature is similar to that of TOI-6894 ( Teff = 3007 K; E. M. Bryant et al. 2025). The spectrum was scaled to repro-duce the bolometric insolation received by TOI-6894 b (5.54 S⊕; E. M. Bryant et al. 2025). According to the companion observational study (M. Zhang et al ., submitted), the inferred atmospheric metallicity of TOI-6894 b lies between 3 × and 10 ×Z⊙.We therefore adopt a fiducial atmospheric model with a metallicity of 3 ×Z⊙, a uniform eddy diffusion coeffi-cient of Kzz = 10 8 cm 2 s−1, and Tint = 100 K. The latter two parameters are representative values of Jupiter’s at-mosphere commonly adopted for gas-giant atmospheres (G. L. Bjoraker et al. 1986; L. Li et al. 2012). 2.4. Sensitivity Analysis 

To assess the sensitivity of the atmospheric chemistry of TOI-6894 b, we computed an additional grid of mod-els using PICASO 2.1 and EPACRIS 2.3, exploring Kzz 

ranging from 10 6 to 10 10 cm 2 s−1, metallicities from 0.1 × to 30 ×Z⊙, and Tint spanning 30 to 400 K. We also adopted the stellar spectrum of AD Leo, an active M4.5 dwarf from the MUSCLES survey III ( Teff = 3400 K; R. O. P. Loyd et al. 2016), scaling it to the same bolometric insolation as TOI-6894 b to assess the im-pact of enhanced UV irradiation. In these sensitivity tests, Kzz = 10 8 cm 2 s−1, [M/H] = 0 (solar metallic-ity), Tint = 100 K, and the GJ 876 stellar spectrum were adopted as the reference model. Chemical kinetic models inherently contain uncertain-ties arising from imperfect knowledge of reaction rate co-efficients. These uncertainties propagate into the model output and therefore represent one of the major caveats in atmospheric chemical modeling. Owing to decades of developments in combustion chemistry, our under-standing of the chemistry of C-H-O species, and to some extent nitrogen chemistry, has been significantly improved through laboratory measurements of key re-actions and ab initio calculations (e.g., W. H. Green Jr 2007; K. Kohse-H¨ oinghaus 2021; J. A. Miller et al. 2021). However, sulfur chemistry remains comparatively poorly constrained because of its chemical complexity and ex-perimental difficulties (A. Stagni et al. 2022). For exam-ple, sulfur-bearing species are often highly reactive and can contaminate or damage chemical kinetic measure-4ment systems (e.g., reactor walls and mass spectrom-eters), making laboratory studies particularly challeng-ing. For this reason, sensitivity analysis is routinely per-formed in chemical engineering applications to evaluate the robustness of chemical kinetic models against uncer-tainties in reaction rate coefficients. Such analyses not only assess the reliability of model predictions but also identify the reactions that exert the strongest control over the chemical system. Accordingly, we evaluated the sensitivity of the model to rate coefficients associated with the major CS 2 forma-tion pathways using the fiducial model (3 ×Z⊙, Kzz =10 8 cm 2 s−1). We first identified the reactions that significantly contribute to CS 2 production and loss at 

P ∼ 0.1 mbar in the atmosphere of TOI-6894 b, cor-responding to the pressure region of primary interest for both photochemistry and JWST observations. We then perturbed each reaction individually by reducing its rate coefficient (i.e., k) by a factor of 10 and per-formed 1D photochemical modeling to examine the re-sulting response in the atmospheric CS 2 abundance at 

P ∼ 0.1 mbar (i.e., [CS 2]). This approach quantifies how uncertainties in the model output (here, [CS 2]) propa-gate from uncertainties in the model input (here, k). The sensitivity is expressed using the normalized CS 2

concentration sensitivity with respect to the rate coeffi-cients, hereafter referred to as the sensitivity coefficient:

After the 1D photochemical models reached steady state, we computed synthetic transmission spectra of TOI-6894 b using the resulting vertical molecular mix-ing ratio profiles with PLATON (M. Zhang et al. 2019). In this work, we used PLATON v6.2 (M. Zhang et al. 2024), which employs molecular opacity tables at a resolving power of R = 20 , 000. We added CS 2 opacities to the default opacity library from M. Zhang et al. (2019) using line lists for all isotopologues from the HITRAN database (I. Gordon et al. 2026). The modeled spectra were then compared to the JWST observations (M. Zhang et al ., submitted). 2.6. Exploring the Dependence of CS 2 Formation on equilibrium temperature ( Teq )

Together with recent CS 2 detections in V1298 Tau e (Teq = 527 K; F. Dai et al. 2026) and WASP-80 b (Teq = 825 K; A. Triantafillides et al. 2026), the detec-tion of CS 2 in TOI-6894 b ( Teq = 418 K) raises the ques-tion of how CS 2 production depends on Teq . To investi-gate this question, we extended our analysis to five ad-ditional temperate-to-warm gas giant exoplanets char-acterized with JWST: TOI-199 b (A. Bello-Arufe et al. 2026), V1298 Tau e (F. Dai et al. 2026), V1298 Tau b (S. Barat et al. 2025), WASP-80 b (A. Triantafillides et al. 2026), and WASP-39 b (Z. Rustamkulov et al. 2023). Together, these planets span a broad range of equilibrium temperatures ( Teq = 350–1116 K) and in-clude both detections and non-detections of CS 2. For each planet, we performed EPACRIS simulations follow-ing the methodology described in Section 2.3 and con-sidered both 1 × and 10 ×Z⊙ metallicities. Details of these models are provided in Appendix A.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS 3.1. Overall Atmospheric Chemistry of TOI-6894 b 

Figure 1 shows the vertical volume mixing ratio (VMR) profiles for the fiducial TOI-6894 b model de-scribed in Section 2.3. In the deep atmosphere ( P ≥ 10 bar), the C-, N-, O-, and S-bearing species are present in their typical forms expected for a H 2-dominated at-mosphere and are readily quenched. Oxygen is primar-ily locked in H 2O, carbon in CH 4, nitrogen in both N 2

and NH 3, and sulfur in H 2S. This behavior reflects the dominance of thermochemical equilibrium in the deep atmosphere, where high temperatures lead to thermo-chemical timescales much shorter than vertical mixing or photochemical timescales (J. I. Moses 2014). In the middle atmosphere ( P ∼ 10 −2 − 1 bar), vertical mix-ing controls the overall composition, producing nearly uniform VMR profiles for the major C-, N-, O-, and S-bearing species. In the upper atmosphere ( P ≲ 10 −2

bar), photochemistry controls the overall composition. A key result is the formation of CS 2 in the upper atmosphere, represented by the magenta solid line in Figure 1. Within the grey-shaded JWST-observable re-gion ( P ∼ 0.1–2 mbar; Z. Rustamkulov et al. 2023), sulfur initially stored as H 2S in the deep atmosphere is progressively converted primarily into CS 2 toward higher altitudes, with smaller fractions forming S 2 and HSSH. Consequently, the upper-atmosphere VMR of CS 2 is approximately a factor of two lower than the deep-atmosphere VMR of H 2S, since each CS 2 molecule contains two sulfur atoms, whereas each H 2S molecule contains only one. This result indicates that sulfur orig-inally locked in H 2S in the deep atmosphere is largely redistributed into CS 2 in the upper atmosphere of TOI-6894 b. Figure 2a shows the sensitivity coefficients of the ma-jor reactions influencing CS 2 formation at P ∼ 0.1 mbar in Figure 1, while Figure 2b presents the dominant re-action pathways from H 2S to CS 2 in the same region. Since CS 2 contains both carbon and sulfur atoms, its formation requires carbon and sulfur reservoirs. In the atmosphere of TOI-6894 b, the dominant carbon source is CH 4, while the dominant sulfur source is H 2S. According to the steady-state atmospheric solution, CH 4 reacts with H atoms to produce CH 3 radicals and H2. Sulfur atoms are liberated from H 2S and subse-quently maintained through a reaction network involv-ing H 2, H, HSS, HSSH, SH, and S 2. Among these, the CH 2SH and H 2CS+H channels dominate, with branching ratios of 34.6% and 64.5%, respectively, whereas the CH 3S channel contributes only 0.9%. Based on the dominant reactions involved in CS 2 for-mation identified through the rate analysis described in Figure 2b and Section 3.2, we subsequently performed sensitivity calculations for each reaction following the procedure described in Section 2.4. The resulting sen-sitivity coefficients are shown in Figure 2a. The model sensitivity is quantified using the sensitivity coefficient (Eqn. 1). Briefly speaking, if the sensitivity coefficient of the CS 2 abundance with respect to reaction i is equal to 1 (i.e., Si, CS 2 =1), reducing the rate coefficient of reac-tion i by a factor of 10 results in a corresponding factor of 10 decrease in the predicted CS 2 abundance, indi-cating that the reaction positively contributes to CS 2

production. Conversely, if Si, CS 2 = −1, reducing the rate coefficient by a factor of 10 increases the predicted CS 2 abundance by a factor of 10, indicating that the reaction suppresses CS 2 formation. In practice, reac-tions with small absolute sensitivity coefficients have a limited influence on the model output. For example, 

|Si, CS 2 | ≤ 0.1 implies that even a factor of 10 pertur-bation in the rate coefficient changes the predicted CS 2

abundance by only ∼20 %, while |Si, CS 2 | ≤ 0.01 corre-sponds to only ∼2 % variation. Figure 2a shows that among the 27 dominant reac-tions associated with CS 2 formation, 25 reactions pos-sess |Si, CS 2 | ≲ 0.05, corresponding to uncertainties of only ≲ 25 % in the predicted CS 2 abundance even un-der order-of-magnitude perturbations to their individual rate coefficients. The SH+H →S+H 2 reaction exhibits the second largest sensitivity ( Si, CS 2 = 0 .1670), corre-sponding to approximately a factor of two variation in CS 2 abundance even if the reaction rate is perturbed by two orders of magnitude. Overall, these results demon-strate the robustness of the predicted CS 2 production against uncertainties in most reaction rates, noting that the order-of-magnitude perturbations adopted here are conservative for many kinetic parameters constrained theoretically or experimentally. 3.4. Sensitivity Analysis of CS 2 Formation to Atmospheric Parameters 

Figure 3 shows the sensitivity of the modeled atmo-sphere of TOI-6894 b to various atmospheric parame-ters, including Kzz , metallicity, and Tint . As shown in Figure 3a, the atmospheric chemistry exhibits only mi-nor variations for Kzz values up to 10 8 cm 2 s−1. How-ever, when a higher vertical mixing coefficient of Kzz =10 10 cm 2 s−1 is assumed, the model predicts significantly lower CS 2 abundances. This behavior arises because stronger vertical mixing reduces the relative contribu-tion of photochemistry within the JWST-observable re-gion (grey shaded region in Figure 3a), leading to a decrease in photochemically produced CS 2 and an en-hanced transport of H 2S from the deeper atmosphere. In other words, the region dominated by vertical mix-ing extends toward higher altitudes and replaces part of the upper atmosphere that is photochemically controlled under lower Kzz conditions. As discussed in Sections 3.2 and 3.3, this further supports our conclusion that CS 2 is primarily a photochemical product in the atmosphere of TOI-6894 b and is preferentially produced in the upper atmosphere where photochemical processes dominate. Varying the atmospheric metallicity primarily controls the maximum abundance of CS 2 formed in the atmo-sphere, whereas the conversion of sulfur from H 2S to CS 2 within the intermediate pressure region ( P ∼ 10 −3–1 bar) exhibits only minor differences, as shown in Fig-ure 3b. Increasing the metallicity enhances the total in-ventories of C, O, and S, leading to higher abundances of CH 4, CO 2, and H 2S. Consequently, the absolute abun-dance of CS 2 increases with metallicity, while the photo-chemical conversion efficiency from H 2S to CS 2 remains largely unchanged. This behavior likely occurs because the photochemistry is primarily controlled by available UV photons in the upper atmosphere rather than by the absolute sulfur inventory. These results suggest that sul-fur in the atmosphere of TOI-6894 b can be efficiently converted into CS 2 via photochemistry over the explored metallicity range from 0.1 × to 30 ×Z⊙.Varying Tint primarily affects the deep atmospheric chemical composition, particularly the abundances of CH 4, CO, and CO 2. The mixing ratios of these species are determined by their thermochemical stability in the deep atmosphere and subsequently quenched by verti-cal mixing, allowing them to remain abundant within the JWST-observable region (J. I. Moses et al. 2011; J. J. Fortney et al. 2020; J. Yang & R. Hu 2024a). As discussed in Section 3.2, CH 4 acts as the primary carbon source for CS 2 formation through photochem-istry. Therefore, the abundance of CS 2 is expected 9to depend on the quenched abundance of deep atmo-spheric CH 4, and consequently on Tint . However, as shown in Figure 3c, the modeled CS 2 abundance exhibits only minor variations within the JWST-observable re-gion over the explored range of Tint = 30–400 K for the 1×Z⊙ atmosphere of TOI-6894 b. This result suggests that the quenched CH 4 abundance above ∼ 100 ppmv (VMR ≳ 10 −4) remains sufficiently high to sustain effi-cient photochemical CS 2 production throughout the Tint 

range of 30–400 K in the atmosphere of TOI-6894 b. For planets with higher Teq and Tint , however, thermochem-ical depletion of CH 4 may eventually limit CS 2 produc-tion, as discussed in Section 3.6. Although not shown, varying the stellar spectrum from GJ 876 to the more UV-active AD Leo enhances CS 2 production deeper ( >1 mbar) in the atmosphere by increasing the availability of 100–400 nm photons and extending the region where photochemistry dominates, increasing CS 2 abundances by up to an order of magni-tude. However, the resulting changes near the ∼1 mbar level most relevant to JWST transmission spectroscopy are comparatively modest, with CS 2 abundances in-creasing by less than a factor of three and producing almost no discernible change in the model transmission spectra (Section 3.5). We therefore omit these results for brevity, as they do not alter our conclusions. Over-all, these findings suggest that our conclusions regarding photochemical CS 2 production are robust to plausible variations in stellar UV activity. 3.5. CONCLUSIONS In this Letter, we performed one-dimensional self-consistent atmospheric modeling of TOI-6894 b, along with an extensive sensitivity analysis to investigate the photochemical production of CS 2. Our models show very good agreement with the JWST NIRSpec/PRISM observations and predict substantial CS 2 abundances in TOI-6894 b’s atmosphere. The formation of CH 4 and H2S in the deep atmosphere is essential for subsequent CS 2 production, since these species serve as the primary carbon and sulfur reservoirs. Through the combined ef-fects of thermochemical and photochemical processes, carbon and sulfur initially stored in CH 4 and H 2S are progressively converted into H 2CS, HCS, CS, and ulti-mately CS 2. Our sensitivity analysis identifies S 2 pho-tolysis as a key process driving the disequilibrium chem-istry that leads to CS 2 formation in TOI-6894 b. By extending our analysis to a broader Teq = 350– 1116 K, we find that detectable CS 2 abundances are favored in temperate-to-warm gas-giant atmospheres (Teq ∼ 400–800 K). This Teq range encompasses 17 gas giant exoplanets that have already been or are planned to be observed with JWST using observing modes sen-sitive to CS 2, making them promising targets for future detections (Figure 5). In particular, our models predict substantial CS 2 production in TOI-6894 b, V1298 Tau e, and WASP-80 b, consistent with the recent detection of CS 2 in these planets (M. Zhang et al. , submitted; A. Triantafillides et al. 2026; F. Dai et al. 2026), as well as the absence of CS 2 in V1298 Tau b (S. Barat et al. 2025). In summary, our modeling results suggest that CS 2 can serve as a valuable tracer of the sulfur inventory in temperate to warm gas giants ( Teq ∼ 400 – 800 K), complementing SO 2, which is expected to be a more effective sulfur tracer in hotter giant exoplanets (S.-M.
