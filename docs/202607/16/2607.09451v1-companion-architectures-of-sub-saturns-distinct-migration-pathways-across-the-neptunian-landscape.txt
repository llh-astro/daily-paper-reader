ABSTRACT 

Context. Close-in sub-Saturns (4–8 .5 R⊕) are distributed unevenly across the period–radius plane: they are depleted in the Neptunian desert, accumulate in a narrow overdensity near P ≃ 3.2–5 .7 d (the Neptunian ridge), and thin out into the more moderately populated savanna at longer periods. 

Aims. We test whether sub-Saturns in di ff erent landscape regions have systematically di ff erent companion architectures, as predicted if desert and ridge planets arrived through high-eccentricity migration while savanna planets migrated quiescently. 

Methods. We compile 90 sub-Saturns in 86 systems with both transit and radial velocity data, construct per-system RV and transit injection-recovery completeness maps and combine them into detection probability surfaces for companions. We then use these combined completeness maps to calculate completeness-corrected companion occurrence rates across di ff erent companion types with a Poisson-Binomial framework.

In both populations, the nearby companions that do exist are almost exclusively small ( M < 20 M ⊕): medium-mass and giant companions within 200 d are rare ( ≲ 14%) everywhere, so the sub-Saturn is typically the dominant body of its inner system. These contrasts are robust to crosscuts in sub-Saturn radius, bulk density, eccentricity, and host-star properties. 

Conclusions. Desert and ridge sub-Saturns reside in dynamically emptied systems whose nearby companion rates match those of hot Jupiters, while savanna sub-Saturns inhabit compact multi-planet systems resembling those of warm Jupiters. This parallel supports two migration channels operating within a single population: high-eccentricity migration delivering planets to the desert and ridge, and quiescent disk migration or in-situ formation populating the savanna.

1. Introduction 

The sub-Saturn exoplanet population (4 R ⊕ ≤ Rp ≤ 8.5 R ⊕)exhibits distinct features that constrain planet formation and evolution, most notably the "Neptune desert" — a prominent paucity of close-in planets (Lecavelier Des Etangs 2007; Szabó & Kiss 2011; Beaugé & Nesvorný 2013; Sanchis-Ojeda et al. 2014a; Lundkvist et al. 2016; Mazeh et al. 2016). Possible ex-planations for this scarcity of planets include atmospheric es-cape (Kurokawa & Nakamoto 2014; Jackson et al. 2017; Owen 2019; Koskinen et al. 2022), migration mechanisms (Matsakos & Königl 2016; Owen & Lai 2018), or a combination of both (Ionov et al. 2018; Owen & Lai 2018; Vissapragada et al. 2022a; Lazovik 2023). The discovery of an overdensity of sub-Saturns at orbital periods between 3.2 and 5.7 days (coined the Neptu-nian ridge, Castro-González et al. 2024a) and a slight underden-sity at orbital periods P > 5.7 days (coined the Neptunian sa-vanna, Bourrier et al. 2023) has complicated the picture of the sub-Saturn population. Recent works have shown that close-in (hot) sub-Saturns in the desert and ridge might have di ff erent properties from the farther-out (warm) sub-Saturns in the sa-

(2024b) analyzed the bulk density of sub-Saturns and found that savanna sub-Saturns have densities that rarely surpass 1 g cm −3 while a significant number of sub-Saturns in the desert and ridge have densities between 1.5 -2.0 g cm −3. Another di ff erence between hot and warm sub-Saturns was found in the metallicity distribution of their host stars. Vissapragada & Behmard (2025) found that the metallic-ity of hot sub-Saturn hosts is significantly higher than for warm sub-Saturns (see also Dong et al. 2018). Furthermore, the host star metallicity of hot sub-Saturns in the desert and ridge is statistically indistinguishable from the host star metallicity of hot Jupiters. Dynamical evidence points in the same direction. Several of the best-characterized planets in the ridge and desert occupy eccentric, near-polar orbits (e.g. HAT-P-11 b, GJ 436 b, GJ 3470 b; Sanchis-Ojeda & Winn 2011; Bourrier et al. 2018; Stefànsson et al. 2022), and close-in Neptunes retain significant eccentricities more often than tidal circularization alone would suggest (Correia et al. 2020). Building on these results, Bourrier et al. (2025) proposed a unified view of the landscape in which low-density Neptunes migrate early within the disk and are fully eroded interior to a density threshold within the ridge, while 

Article number, page 1 of 25 arXiv:2607.09451v1 [astro-ph.EP] 10 Jul 2026 A&A proofs: manuscript no. aanda 

denser planets arrive late in the desert and ridge through high-eccentricity migration (HEM). Castro-González et al. (2026) showed that HEM followed by tidal circularization naturally re-produces the location of the ridge itself. Given the similarity in host star metallicity as well as the coin-cidence between the Neptunian ridge and the hot Jupiter pile-up (Udry et al. 2003), Vissapragada & Behmard (2025) sug-gest that sub-Saturns in the desert and ridge might form "top-down". These sub-Saturns would then be the exposed interiors of hot Jupiters that have undergone atmospheric escape. That would naturally explain the higher bulk densities of ridge and desert sub-Saturns. This scenario requires an escape mechanism more e ffi cient than photoevaporation alone, which is not e ffi cient enough in stripping planets at the upper edge of the Neptunian desert of their atmosphere to produce smaller planets (Vissapra-gada et al. 2022b; Osborn et al. 2023; Thomas et al. 2025a). Hot Jupiters that migrated via high-eccentricity migration could be subjected to partial envelope disruption during close pericenter passage (Faber et al. 2005; Owen & Lai 2018). An alternative way for hot Jupiters that undergo a quiescent migration to lose a significant portion of their atmosphere is through Roche Lobe overflow (Valsecchi et al. 2014, 2015; Jackson et al. 2016). It is not out of the question that hot sub-Saturns form similarly to the warm sub-Saturns, i.e., not undergoing or prematurely stopping runaway accretion, explaining their smaller size com-pared to the Jovian planets (e.g., Ida & Lin 2004; Suzuki et al. 2018; Moldenhauer et al. 2021; Schlecker et al. 2021, 2022). Ob-servational support for this interior-structure perspective comes from the envelope mass fractions of sub-Saturns. Thomas et al. (2025b) found their distribution to be bimodal, plausibly mark-ing the onset of runaway gas accretion and dividing the pop-ulation into Neptune-like planets with modest envelopes and Jovian-like planets dominated by their gaseous envelope. The ridge being at the same periods as the hot Jupiter pile-up could just be a consequence of the specific migration mechanism, mir-roring the one of hot Jupiters, without the sub-Saturns evolv-ing directly from evaporating hot Jupiters. A higher core mass and atmospheric metallicity would help these sub-Saturns sur-vive photoevaporation from their host stars through deeper grav-itational wells and more e ffi cient atmospheric cooling (Owen & Murray-Clay 2018), which might produce the observed density and host star metallicity distributions. In this work, we use the companion architectures of sub-Saturn systems as an additional, dynamical tracer of their formation and migration pathways. The three scenarios outlined above make distinct predictions for the systems’ present-day architectures. High-eccentricity migration is dynamically violent: the migrat-ing planet crosses the orbits of any nearby siblings and typically ejects or accretes them, so HEM predicts a deficit of nearby com-panions and low system multiplicity (Rasio & Ford 1996; Wu & Lithwick 2011; Mustill et al. 2015; Petrovich 2015; Bitsch et al. 2020). The paucity of nearby companions to hot Jupiters is widely read as evidence for exactly this history (Latham et al. 2011; Ste ff en et al. 2012; Knutson et al. 2014; Dawson & John-son 2018). Quiescent disk migration or in-situ formation, in con-trast, preserves compact, dynamically cold multi-planet config-urations like those of the Kepler multis (Goldreich & Tremaine 1980; Ida & Lin 2008; Dittkrist et al. 2014; Weiss et al. 2018). This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the sub-Saturn sample. Section 3 presents the RV, transit, and com-bined detection completeness analysis. Section 4 presents the completeness-corrected occurrence rates across the landscape and their crosscuts in planetary and stellar properties. We discuss the implications for sub-Saturn migration and the hot Jupiter connection in Sect. 5 and summarize our conclusions in Sect. 6. 

2. Sub-Saturn sample 

For our sample, we queried the NASA Exoplanet Archive com-posite table (Christiansen et al. 2025) 1 for sub-Saturns with radii and masses measured to a precision of 33 % or better. In ac-cordance with Castro-González et al. (2024a), which defined the boundaries of the Neptune desert, ridge, and savanna, we con-sider all planets with radii between 4 R⊕ and 8 .5 R⊕ as sub-Saturns. The precision requirement is set to make certain that only well-characterized sub-Saturns are in the sample, limiting the probability of false positives while also ensuring that there is su ffi cient photometric and spectroscopic data to detect compan-ion planets. In total, our sample consists of 90 sub-Saturns orbit-ing 86 host systems, with four systems hosting two sub-Saturns each. We classify each sub-Saturn according to its position in the exo-Neptunian landscape defined by Castro-González et al. (2024a): the Neptunian desert ( P < 3.2 d), the Neptunian ridge (3 .2 ≤ P ≤ 5.7 d), and the Neptunian savanna ( P > 5.7 d). Our sample contains 9 desert, 29 ridge, and 52 savanna sub-Saturns, with four two-sub-Saturn systems contributing counts to the re-spective bins of each planet individually. Figure 1 shows the system architectures of our sample. Only 1 of the 9 desert sub-Saturns has a known companion (11%). Out of the 29 ridge sub-Saturns, 4 have companions (14%), and from the 52 savanna sub-Saturns, 30 are in multi-planet sys-tems (58%). The companion rate thus increases sharply from the desert and ridge to the savanna (Almenara et al. in prep.). However, this trend might be an artifact of observational biases. Planets that are close to their host stars are easier to detect and therefore require a shorter baseline and lower precision in their observations. Therefore, it is possible that the lack of compan-ions for the close-in desert and ridge sub-Saturns is due to the limited sensitivity of the available data. In order to correct for this bias, we analyze the completeness of the photometric and spectroscopic datasets for each sub-Saturn in our sample.

> 3.1. RV completeness

We collected all available published radial velocity (RV) mea-surements for each of the 86 sub-Saturn systems. Table G.1 sum-marizes the number of RV points for each system. > 3.2. Transit Completeness

All 86 systems in our sample lie within the TESS (Ricker et al. 2015) footprint and have been observed in at least one TESS 

sector, providing a minimum baseline of ∼27 d per system. In addition, 10 systems were observed by the Kepler (Borucki et al. 2010a) primary mission with a nearly continuous four-year baseline, and 9 systems were observed by the K2 mis-sion with ∼80-day campaign baselines. We construct separate photometric completeness maps for each mission, yielding a to-tal of 105 completeness surfaces. Light curves were retrieved from the Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes (MAST) us-ing the lightkurve package (Lightkurve Collaboration et al. 2018). For each system and mission, we downloaded the Pre-search Data Conditioning Simple Aperture Photometry (PDC-SAP) flux, which has been corrected for instrumental system-atics by the respective mission pipelines (Smith et al. 2012; Stumpe et al. 2012, 2014). For the TESS photometry, we used the 120 s cadence data from the Science Processing Operations Center (SPOC; Jenkins et al. 2016) for all sectors where it was available; otherwise, we used the full-frame image (FFI) extrac-tions (Caldwell et al. 2020; Huang et al. 2020a,b). For Kepler 

targets, we downloaded the long-cadence ( ∼30 min) PDCSAP light curves for all available quarters (Q1–Q17). For K2 tar-gets, we downloaded the standard K2 PDCSAP light curves for the relevant campaigns. Before injecting synthetic planet sig-nals, we masked the signals of all known transiting planets in each system. We opted to mask the signals instead of subtracting them in order to avoid biases from imperfect subtraction. The masked light curve was then detrended using the biweight time-windowed filter implemented in the wotan package (Hippke et al. 2019), with a filter window of 0 .5 d. We injected Ninj = 10 ,000 synthetic transit signals into each detrended light curve. The parameters of each injected planet were drawn independently from a log-uniform distribution in the range P ∈ [0 .5, 1000] d and Rp ∈ [0 .5, 20] R⊕, converted to Rp/R⋆. Impact parameters were drawn from a uniform dis-tribution b ∈ [0 , 1], and the transit epochs were distributed uniformly within one orbital period from the start of observa-tions. Each synthetic transit was computed using batman (Krei-dberg 2015) and injected into the masked and detrended light curves. We attempted to recover these signals through a blind Box Least Squares (BLS; Kovács et al. 2002) transit search us-ing the astropy implementation (Astropy Collaboration et al. 2013). For each injection, the BLS periodogram was computed over a period range of [ Pinj × 0.37 , Pinj × 2.25] with 5000 fre-quency steps, and a grid of trial transit durations spanning 0 .5– 2× the injected duration. The edges of the period grid are chosen to avoid having a harmonic of the period at the edge of the BLS. An injection was considered recovered if the peak BLS signal satisfied a signal-to-pink noise ratio S /Npink ≥ 8 (Hartman & Bakos 2016; Vach et al. 2024) and the recovered period matched the injected period to within 5% (including checks at the 1 /2, 2/1, 1 /3, and 3 /1 harmonics). 

> 3.3. Combined Completeness

The occurrence-rate framework of Sect. 3.4 interprets each sys-tem’s detections and non-detections against the sensitivity of their available datasets. Since the known companions were dis-covered through either channel (e.g. non-transiting outer com-panions through their RV signals alone, and small close-in com-panions through their transits alone) the relevant quantity is the probability that a hypothetical companion would have been re-covered by at least one of the two datasets. The two methods moreover probe complementary regions of the companion pa-rameter space: the photometry provides sensitivity to small plan-ets at short orbital periods, where RV detection is limited by the measurement precision, while the RV data retain sensitivity at long periods, where the geometric transit probability vanishes. For these reasons, we combined the RV and transit completeness surfaces into a single completeness map per system, quantifying the detectability of companions for each sub-Saturn. The RV completeness maps are defined in ( P, K) space, while the transit completeness maps are defined in ( P, Rp) space. For each system j in our sample and each point on a grid in com-panion parameter space ( P, Mp), we drew Nmc = 1000 synthetic companions. 3.4. Occurrence rate calculation 

We compute completeness-corrected companion occurrence rates using a Poisson-Binomial detection model. This frame-work models the expected number of companions per system as a Poisson random variable and derives the detection prob-ability for the binary outcome (whether at least one compan-ion was detected) as a function of the survey completeness. In contrast to other binary occurrence rate approaches, such as the Beta-Binomial framework (e.g., Bryan & Lee 2024), this ap-proach correctly accounts for the fact that systems hosting multi-ple companions in a given parameter space region have a higher probability of yielding at least one detection, an e ff ect that is particularly relevant for the savanna sub-Saturns in our sample. For each companion category (e.g. nearby companions with 

P < 200 d), we model the true number of companions in sys-tem j as k j ∼ Poisson( μ), where μ is the expected number of companions per system in that category, assumed to be the same for all systems within a given population bin. 4.2. Companion rates by sub-Saturn radius 

Recently, the analysis of envelope mass fractions of sub-Saturns by Thomas et al. (2025b) revealed a bimodal distribution which might originate from the onset of runaway gas accretion, divid-ing the sub-Saturn population into Neptune-like and Jovian plan-ets. To test whether the planetary multiplicity depends on the sub-Saturn size, we split the sample at Rp = 6 R⊕ into a Neptune-like bin (4 R⊕ ≤ Rp < 6 R⊕, N = 44 .5, 46 planets) and a giant-like bin (6 R⊕ ≤ Rp ≤ 8.5 R⊕, N = 41 .5, 44 planets) according to the radius boundaries that corresponded to the envelope mass fraction gap in Thomas et al. (2025b). Comparing the two radius classes (Appendix B), we find that they exhibit very similar companion rates across most categories. The occurrence rates for all companion classes agree within 1 σ

between the Neptune-like and giant-like sub-Saturns. When we further subdivide by landscape (Figure 4), the landscape gradient remains the dominant signal in both radius bins with insignifi-cant di ff erences between the radius classes within a given land-scape. This indicates that the landscape-dependent multiplicity trend is not an artifact of one particular sub-Saturn size dominat-ing a given landscape and it persists independently of the planet radius.

5. Discussion 

5.1. Implications for the migration of sub-Saturns 

The results of our occurrence rate analysis support di ff erent evo-lutionary origins for the desert +ridge and savanna sub-Saturns. Sub-Saturns in the savanna have companion architectures con-sistent with dynamically cold migration with a nearby com-panion rate of 69 .9+6.9

−7.7% and a multiple companion rate of 64 .4+8.9

−9.8%. We might expect companions that orbit much closer to their host star than the sub-Saturn to survive HEM from the savanna sub-Saturns if they do not come close enough to dis-rupt the orbit of the companion. However, the sub-Saturn should at least clear planets whose orbit it crosses during dynamical migration and thus they should lack close companions that or-bit outside of the present-day sub-Saturn orbit. In our analysis, the nearby companions are found to be orbiting both inside and outside the sub-Saturn’s orbit at similar occurrence rates. Thus, these systems are consistent with formation in a dynamically quiescent protoplanetary disk, either in situ or through gradual disk-driven migration. The population of sub-Saturns residing

Because the landscape classes are defined solely by the or-bital period of the sub-Saturn, the deficit of the desert and ridge planets might reflect a generic scarcity of companions to short-period planets instead of tracing their migration history. The broader close-in planet population argues against a generic scarcity. Small planets on comparably short or even shorter or-bits are routinely accompanied: ultra-short-period planets ( P <

Likewise, super-Earths and sub-Neptunes with orbital periods inside the ridge boundary fre-quently reside in the compact, dynamically cold configurations characteristic of the Kepler multi-planet systems (Weiss et al. 2018; Zhu et al. 2018). A short present-day orbit alone therefore does not imply the absence of nearby companions. Instead, the companion deficit at P ≲ 5.7 d appears specific to intermediate-mass and giant planets (i.e. desert /ridge sub-Saturns and hot Jupiters) which points to a mass-dependent delivery mechanism, such as HEM, that operates on these bodies while the small-planet population reaches similar periods with its companions intact. While our analysis implies these planets to be rare for all sub-Saturns there are two caveats which need to be con-sidered. First, our massive outer perturber category only extends to orbital periods of 10,000 days, which was chosen due to the reduced sensitivity at longer orbital periods. However, a mas-sive perturber could reside in farther orbits which are not con-sidered in our analysis. Second, this category is by construc-tion restricted to planetary companions: three of the 38 systems hosting a desert or ridge sub-Saturn (NGTS-14 A, TOI-762 A, TOI-3984 A) and eight of the 49 hosting a savanna sub-Saturn are known to host at least one stellar companion, a fraction of which may themselves be capable of driving high-eccentricity migration through von Zeipel–Lidov–Kozai cycles (Fabrycky & Tremaine 2007; Naoz 2016), as invoked for hot Jupiters. As such the rate of perturbers capable of driving HEM is likely higher than our occurrence rate of massive companions and thus does not necessarily rule out that desert and ridge planets preferably migrate via HEM. Another key finding of this work is that the landscape gra-dient in nearby companion occurrence is driven almost entirely by small ( M < 20 M ⊕) planets. Medium (20–80 M ⊕) and giant (> 80 M ⊕) companions within 200 days are rare and statistically indistinguishable between the desert +ridge and savanna popula-tions. This indicates that the sub-Saturn is typically the dominant body in its immediate orbital neighborhood, regardless of land-scape. The distinguishing factor is whether small super-Earth and sub-Neptune companions coexist with the sub-Saturn. In the disk migration scenario, the sub-Saturn forms alongside smaller planets in a common protoplanetary disk, preserving a hierarchy of planet sizes. In the HEM scenario, the dynamical excitation disrupts or ejects these fragile small companions (Mustill et al. 2015), while the massive perturber responsible for triggering the migration resides at long periods and is largely una ff ected. To test whether the landscape gradient is driven by a con-founding variable rather than reflecting a genuine architectural di ff erence, we subdivide the sample by sub-Saturn radius, bulk density, eccentricity, host star metallicity, and host star mass, and repeat the occurrence rate analysis within each bin. The savanna companion rate exceeds that of the desert +ridge within every bin, with no crosscut variable eliminating or revers-ing the trend. > 5.3. The radial velocity follow-up selection function

In order to achieve maximum sensitivity to detect companion planets, we required the planets in our sample to have both tran-sit and RV data instead of the usual approach of focusing on one homogeneous dataset. This introduces a potential selection eff ect: the sample is shaped not only by which planets exist, but by which transiting candidates were chosen for RV follow-up. If the decision to pursue RV follow-up depended on system multiplicity, for example, if multi-planet systems were prefer-entially targeted because they are scientifically richer, or con-versely avoided because they require more observations to char-acterize, then the multiplicity distribution of our sample would not represent the underlying population, biasing our companion occurrence rates. We compared our sample against the parent population of all transit-detected sub-Saturn hosts in our radius range (4 ≤

Rp ≤ 8.5 R⊕), without the mass-precision requirement, yielding 294 host systems of which 86 (29%) have su ffi cient RV data to enter our sample. The follow-up fraction shows no positive de-pendence on transiting multiplicity. In fact we find the opposite: 32% of hosts with a single transiting planet versus 25% of multi-transiting hosts are followed up (standardized mean di ff erence 

−0.16), and a permutation Cochran–Armitage trend test gives 

p = 0.45. The mild multiplicity imbalance traces to the discov-ery survey, Kepler hosts being both multi-rich and faint. To con-trol for such confounding we fit an unpenalized logistic regres-sion of follow-up status on a binary multiplicity indicator (two or more transiting planets), the host-star Gaia magnitude, the sub-Saturn orbital period and radius, and the discovery survey. The follow-up probability depends strongly on host-star brightness (p < 10 −4) and on the sub-Saturn properties ( p = 0.005 for or-bital period, p = 0.011 for radius), as expected: brighter stars, shorter periods, and larger planets yield larger and more read-ily confirmed RV signals. Crucially, the multiplicity coe ffi cient is consistent with zero ( p = 0.86), corresponding to a multi-versus-single follow-up odds ratio of 1 .07 with a 95% confidence interval of [0 .50 , 2.30]. We further note that the residual raw im-balance points in the conservative direction: an under-selection of multi-planet systems would deflate the savanna companion rates and cause us to underestimate the landscape gradient. To propagate the remaining uncertainty in the selection function into the occurrence rates, we constructed inverse-probability weights that remove the multiplicity-dependent part of the fitted selection model at fixed observability, w j =

ˆπ(x j; βmulti →0) /ˆπ(x j), and repeated the Poisson-Binomial infer-ence with these weights combined with the 1 /nss weights of Sect. 3.4. Only the multiplicity channel is reweighted: the de-pendence of follow-up on brightness and planet size acts on ob-servability, which is precisely what the per-system completeness maps already quantify and correct. The measured correction is negligible with every category shifting by less than 1 percent-age point and propagating the selection-model uncertainty by bootstrap resampling of the parent sample (200 refits) bounds the 68% systematic within ±6 ( ±5) percentage points for the sa-vanna (desert +ridge) categories, well inside the statistical uncer-tainties. Even under stress scenarios in which multi-planet sys-tems are assumed to be 1 .5 or 2 times more likely to receive follow-up, the largest shift is 17 percentage points (the savanna nearby rate under the 2 × scenario), leaving the landscape gradi-ent intact. These results show that the RV follow-up selection function operates on observability (i.e. stellar brightness, orbital period, and planet size) rather than on system architecture. Since these observability factors are what our per-system completeness maps quantify and correct for, we conclude that the follow-up selec-tion does not significantly bias our occurrence rate analysis. The strong dependence of follow-up on sub-Saturn radius is further reassuring in light of our finding (Sect. 4.2) that companion oc-currence is itself independent of sub-Saturn radius: even though larger sub-Saturns are over-represented in our sample, this does not distort the inferred companion rates.

We have analyzed the companion architectures of 90 sub-Saturns (4–8.5 R⊕) in 86 host systems across the Neptunian desert, ridge, and savanna, using combined radial velocity and transit com-pleteness maps to derive completeness-corrected companion oc-currence rates with a Poisson-Binomial detection model. Appendix A: Validation and robustness of the occurrence rate methodology 

The completeness-corrected occurrence rates of this work rest on three methodological choices: the statistical framework used for the inference (the Poisson-Binomial model of Sect. 3.4), the prior on the Poisson rate μ, and the assumed intrinsic distribution of undetected companions within each category region. This ap-pendix stress-tests all three. We re-derive the occurrence rates with three independent statistical methods (Appendix A.1), un-der three di ff erent priors (Appendix A.2), and under three as-sumed intrinsic distributions (Appendix A.3). The population comparisons that carry the conclusions of this paper are un-changed in every case. 

> Appendix A.1: Independent statistical methods

We validate the Poisson-Binomial results with three independent methods, each of which relaxes a di ff erent assumption of the pri-mary framework. 

Beta-Binomial. Following Bryan & Lee (2024), occurrence is treated as a binary per-system outcome: system j contributes an e ff ective number of trials w jC j, and the posterior of the de-tected fraction is a Beta distribution with the weighted detections and e ff ective trials as counts, f ∼ Beta( ndet + 1, neff − ndet + 1). Because no count distribution is assumed, this method is insen-sitive to the Poisson assumption of the main framework. The agreement of the Beta-Binomial comparisons with the Poisson-Binomial ones therefore shows that this assumption does not drive our conclusions. In bins with low completeness but a large number of detected companions, the detections can exceed the eff ective trials ( ndet > neff , e.g. 28 .5 versus ≃ 20 for the sa-vanna any-companion category), a regime in which the binary eff ective-trials model is internally inconsistent; in these cases we floor the second Beta parameter at unity, so the corresponding entries in Table A.1 should be read as upper edges of the frame-work’s validity. 

Poisson-process likelihood. Following Rosenthal et al. (2022), companions are modelled as a Poisson process with con-stant intensity Λ per unit (log P, log M) area; the likelihood com-bines the total weighted detection count with the summed ef-fective area P j w j C j A j. The occurrence fraction of system j is 

f j = 1 − e−ΛA j , where A j is the area of the category region for that system. 

Approximate Bayesian Computation. ABC-PMC (Kunimoto & Matthews 2020) is a likelihood-free method that estimates the posterior by simulating synthetic surveys. Our forward model simulates the same generative process as the Poisson-Binomial likelihood: for a proposed rate μ drawn from a uniform prior, each system receives k j ∼ Poisson( μ) companions placed uni-formly in (log P, log M) within the category region, and each companion is detected with the probability given by the com-bined completeness map at its individual location. The summary statistic is the 1 /nss -weighted number of systems with at least one (at least two for the multiple-companion category) detected companion. Because the completeness enters at the simulated companion positions rather than through the regional mean C j,ABC provides a direct internal check that reducing the two-dimensional completeness map to the single scalar C j loses no information relevant to the inference. Table A.1 compares the four methods. The ordering and sig-nificance of the landscape contrast are preserved throughout. 

P( fsav > fd+r) agrees to within 0 .01 between all four methods for the any, nearby, and multiple companion categories, and the mas-sive long-period category is consistent between the populations under every method ( P = 0.40–0 .44). The absolute rates dif-fer systematically, in the high-rate, low-completeness categories. These o ff sets have understood origins. The Beta-Binomial un-derestimates the e ff ective detection probability of systems that host several companions in the same region, biasing its fractions upward in the savanna nearby and small-companion categories (83 .3% vs the Poisson-Binomial 69 .9% for savanna nearby com-panions); for the multiple-companion category the same binary treatment acts in the opposite direction, since detecting the ≥ 2outcome requires two successes rather than one, and the Beta-Binomial fraction falls below the Poisson-Binomial there (56 .8% vs 64 .4%). The Poisson-process likelihood weights all detections equally regardless of the completeness of the system they occur in, biasing its fractions downward (57 .4% for the savanna nearby bin). The two methods therefore bracket the Poisson-Binomial from above and below for the categories requiring at least one companion. ABC tracks the Poisson-Binomial medians closely in every well-populated category (70 .7% vs 69 .9% for savanna nearby companions; 34 .3% vs 33 .1% for desert +ridge any com-panions), directly validating the reduction of the completeness map to its regional mean. We conclude that the Poisson-Binomial framework provides the most appropriate estimates for our sample, as it conditions on each system’s individual outcome and completeness and cor-rectly handles the multi-companion systems that dominate the savanna population; the landscape gradient in companion occur-rence is confirmed by all independent methods. 

> Appendix A.2: Prior sensitivity

The Poisson-Binomial posterior depends on the prior placed on the rate μ, and the dependence is strongest where the likelihood is weakest: in bins where the product of detections and mean completeness is small, the likelihood is nearly flat over a decade in μ, and the prior’s mass at high occurrence fractions can dom-inate the posterior median.
