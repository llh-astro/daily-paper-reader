Abstract The total dust mass of protoplanetary disks is a key property that determines the potential for planet formation. Upper Scorpius (USco), with an age of ∼ 5 − 12 Myr, provides an important laboratory for investigating the evolution of dust reservoirs on timescales comparable to those of planet formation. In this work, we analyze the dust mass distribution of 136 full and transitional disks in USco using the largest ALMA 0.88 mm continuum sample currently available for the region. To improve the accuracy of dust mass estimates, we construct a grid of 918 self-consistent radiative transfer models that account for stellar-mass-dependent disk sizes, accretion rates, and porous dust properties. The models yield a new calibration between dust temperature and stellar luminosity, predicting systematically higher dust temperatures for low-mass stars than commonly adopted prescriptions. Using these revised dust temperatures together with porous dust opacities, we derive dust masses for the USco disks and compare them with those of a younger sample in the Chamaeleon I star-forming region. We find a median dust mass of 0.95 𝑀 ⊕ for the USco disks, approximately six times lower than that of the Chamaeleon I disks (5 .69 𝑀 ⊕), providing strong evidence for substantial depletion of millimeter-sized dust grains over the first several Myr of disk evolution. We confirm the previous finding of a highly significant correlation between stellar mass and dust mass, with a slightly steeper relation in USco than in Chamaeleon I. The low dust masses observed in USco, together with their comparison to mature exoplanetary systems, suggest that a large fraction of the primordial solid reservoir has already been incorporated into larger bodies, removed by radial drift, or hidden from millimeter observations by ages of ∼ 5 − 12 Myr. 

Key words: stars: planetary systems — protoplanetary disks — radiative transfer 

1 INTRODUCTION 

Protoplanetary disks composed of gas and dust are the birthplace of planets (e.g., Williams & Cieza 2011 ; Raymond & Morbidelli 2022 ; Drążkowska et al. 2023 ). As the initial building blocks of planets, submicron-sized dust grains in protoplanetary disks collide and coagulate into larger aggregates, even-tually forming pebbles, planetesimals, and planetary embryos ( Lissauer 1993 ; Dullemond & Dominik 2005 ; Birnstiel et al. 2016 ). Dust grains also strongly influence the thermal and chemical structure of disks by regulating the opacity and mediating radiative heating and cooling processes (e.g., Dullemond et al. 2007 ; Henning & Semenov 2013 ). In addition, dust particles provide the surface area on which chemical reactions can occur to potentially form complex organic molecules ( Garrod & Herbst 2006 ; Öberg et al. arXiv:2607.14850v1 [astro-ph.EP] 16 Jul 2026 2 Deng et al. 

2023 ). Therefore, constraining the dust content in protoplanetary disks is essential not only for un-derstanding the efficiency and timescale of planet formation, but also for providing key observational constraints on models of grain growth, radial drift, and disk chemistry ( Birnstiel 2024 ). The total amount of dust material in protoplanetary disks is one of the most important properties that characterize the potential for planet formation. (Sub-)millimeter continuum observations is the primary method for estimating dust masses (e.g., Andrews 2020 ; Miotello et al. 2023 ). The advent of the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) has revolutionized this field by sensitive continuum surveys of nearby star-forming regions, for instance Taurus ( Long et al. 2019 ), Lupus ( Ansdell et al. 2016 ), Chamaeleon I ( Pascucci et al. 2016 ), Ophiuchus ( Williams et al. 2019 ), and Upper Scorpius ( Barenfeld et al. 2016 ). These surveys have revealed a systematic decline in dust masses with the age of star-forming regions, indicating substantial dust evolution over Myr timescales (e.g., 

Andrews et al. 2013 ; Pascucci et al. 2016 ; Grant et al. 2021 ; Manara et al. 2023 ). However, significant uncertainties remain in dust mass estimates, particularly in terms of the adopted dust temperature and dust opacity ( Beckwith & Sargent 1991 ; Miotello et al. 2023 ; Liu et al. 2024 ). Refining these assumptions is therefore important for obtaining more accurate constraints on the solid mass budget available for planet formation. Upper Scorpius association (hereafter USco) is a benchmark region for investigating the evolution of protoplanetary disks. As part of the Scorpius-Centaurus OB association, USco has an estimated age of 5 − 12 Myr ( Pecaut et al. 2012 ; David et al. 2019 ; Luhman & Esplin 2020 ), which is substantially older than other nearby star-forming regions such as Taurus (1 − 3 Myr, Krolikowski et al. 2021 ) and Chamaeleon I (1 − 2 Myr, Galli et al. 2021 ). At this evolutionary stage, disks are expected to have under-gone significant dust evolution and dispersal ( Williams & Cieza 2011 ; Testi et al. 2014 ). Consequently, USco provides an ideal laboratory for studying the evolution of dust reservoirs on timescales com-parable to those of planet formation. The disk population in USco has been extensively surveyed at (sub-)millimeter wavelengths. Carpenter et al. (2014 ) first presented ALMA 0.88 mm continuum obser-vations of 20 K- and M-type stars hosting protoplanetary disks. The sample was subsequently expanded to 106 disks by Barenfeld et al. (2016 ). More recently, Carpenter et al. (2025 ) carried out a new ALMA 0.88 mm continuum survey and increased the sample to 202 systems, providing the most comprehensive census of the USco disk population to date. In this work, based on the expanded sample compiled by Carpenter et al. (2025 ), we analyze the dust masses of USco disks by incorporating updated dust temperatures derived from physically motivated disk models and porous dust opacities. We investigate the statistical properties of the dust mass distribution, and discuss their implications for dust evolution and planet formation. Sect. 2 describes the sample selection and the derivation of the host stellar properties. In Sect. 3, we construct a grid of self-consistent radiative transfer models that account for stellar-mass-dependent disk size and accretion rate, with the goal of calibrating the relation between stellar luminosity and dust temperature over a broad range of stellar types. Sect. 4 presents the inferred dust masses and their statistical properties. Finally, Section 5

summarizes our main results.

3 DUST TEMPERATURE AS A FUNCTION OF STELLAR LUMINOSITY 

As given in Eq. 1, one needs to know the dust temperature ( 𝑇 dust ) to calculate the dust mass. A value of 20 K is a common choice in literature studies (e.g., Pascucci et al. 2016 ; Manara et al. 2023 ). However, as the stellar irradiation is the main heating source for protoplanetary disks ( Chiang & Goldreich 1997 ;

Dullemond et al. 2007 ), especially in the Class II evolutionary stage, dust temperature should in principle vary with stellar parameters. Based on a set of radiative transfer models, Andrews et al. (2013 ) obtained a relation between stellar luminosity and dust temperature 𝑇 dust = 25 (𝐿 ★/𝐿 ⊙ )0.25 K. The scaling of 𝑇 dust 

with 𝐿 ★ was further investigated for disks around low-mass stars and brown dwarfs (e.g., Daemgen et al. 2016 ; van der Plas et al. 2016 ; Hendler et al. 2017 ), and the relation, 𝑇 dust = 22 (𝐿 ★/𝐿 ⊙ )0.16 K, appears flatter than that suggested by Andrews et al. (2013 ). The studies discussed above have substantially improved our understanding of the dependence of dust temperature on stellar luminosity. Nevertheless, several important issues remain. First, most previous investigations assume a fixed disk outer radius (typically 100 AU) across the entire range of stellar masses. However, spatially resolved ALMA observations show that disks around lower-mass stars are systematically smaller than those around higher-mass stars (e.g., Tripathi et al. 2017 ; Andrews et al. 2018 ; Andrews 2020 ). Because disk size determines the characteristic distance of dust grains from the central heating source, it has a direct impact on the dust temperature ( van der Plas et al. 2016 ;

Hendler et al. 2017 ). Second, viscous heating is often neglected in the radiative transfer modeling. Observations indicate that the disk accretion rate generally decreases with stellar mass (e.g., Fang et al. 2009 ; Manara et al. 2023 ). Although low-mass stars and brown dwarfs generally exhibit lower accretion rates, the contribution of accretion luminosity to the disk energy budget may still be non-negligible because their stellar luminosities are also substantially lower. Third, recent infrared and millimeter observations suggest that dust grains in protoplanetary disks might be highly porous (e.g., Ginski et al. 2023 ; Tazaki et al. 2023 ; Zhang et al. 2023 ). Porous dust grains differ a lot from compact grains in terms of scattering and absorption behaviors, which in turn have a direct impact on the dust temperature and the inferred dust mass ( Kirchschlager et al. 2019 ; Brunngräber & Wolf 2021 ; Liu et al. 2024 ). In this section, we recalibrate the relation between stellar luminosity and dust temperature by generating a grid of radiative transfer models computed with the HOCHUNK3D code ( Whitney et al. 2013 ). The model grid spans a broad range of stellar masses, from intermediate-mass stars to planetary-mass objects. Unlike previous studies, our models incorporate stellar-mass-dependent disk sizes and accretion rates, allowing us to assess their impact on the dust temperature over a wide range of stellar luminosities.

3.2 Dust properties 

The dust composition was adopted from the DSHARP model ( Birnstiel et al. 2018 ), which is composed of water ice ( Warren & Brandt 2008 ), astronomical silicates ( Draine 2003 ), troilite ( Henning & Stognienko 1996 ), and refractory organic material ( Henning & Stognienko 1996 ), with volume fractions being 36%, 17%, 3%, and 44%, respectively. To introduce porosity, we mixed the optical constants of the DSHARP dust model with vacuum using the Bruggeman mixing rule ( Bruggeman 1935 ). The mixed refractive indices were used to calculate dust absorption and scattering properties with the Mie theory and OpTool 

(Dominik et al. 2021 ). The volume fraction of vacuum is controlled by the porosity P. We chose P = 0.8that is consistent with observational constraints of protoplanetary disks (e.g., Zhang et al. 2023 ) and the cometary nucleus 67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko ( Kofman et al. 2015 ; Jorda et al. 2016 ). The bulk density of the porous dust grains is 𝜌 s = 0.34 g /cm 3. We note that compact dust particles can be considered as a limiting case of P = 0. We assumed that the grain size distribution follows the power law d 𝑛 (𝑎 ) ∝ 𝑎 −3.5d𝑎 with a minimum grain size fixed to 𝑎 min = 0.01 𝜇 m. For the SGP, the maximum grain size was set to 𝑎 max = 1 𝜇 m. For the LGP, we adopted 𝑎 max = 1 mm accounting for the effect of grain growth that is commonly seen in protoplanetary disks (e.g., Liu et al. 2017 ; Li et al. 2023 ; Shi et al. 2026 ). By taking 𝑎 min = 0.01 𝜇 mand 𝑎 max = 1 mm in the calculation, porous grains with P = 0.8 feature an absorption coefficient at 𝜆 = 0.88 mm of 𝜅 0.88mm = 0.84 cm 2/g, which is about 4 times lower than compact grains with

The disk outer radius 𝑅 out , which is linked with the characteristic radius via 𝑅 out = 5 𝑅 c, is a crucial parameter affecting the temperature structure. ALMA observations reveal that 𝑅 out is stellar-mass dependent ( Tripathi et al. 2017 ; Andrews 2020 ), with smaller disks toward lower mass stars. By analyzing millimeter continuum images, Andrews et al. (2018 ) determined the effective disk radius 𝑅 eff 

(i.e., the radius enclosing 68% of the total flux) for 105 nearby protoplanetary disks. They obtained a linear correlation between the effective disk radius and stellar mass log 𝑅 eff = 1.77 + 0.58 log 𝑀 ★. As demonstrated in Liu et al. (2024 ), the characteristic radius is a good representative for the effective disk radius. Therefore, we directly sampled 𝑅 c using the relation provided by Andrews et al. (2018 ). For a typical T Tauri star with 𝑀 ★ = 0.5 𝑀 ⊙ , the characteristic radius is 𝑅 c ∼ 40 AU. The disk accretion rate directly determines the total energy release due to viscous heating. Andrews et al. (2013 ) and van der Plas et al. (2016 ) arises from the updated disk structure adopted in this work, including the stellar-mass-dependent disk sizes. As discussed above, variations in the flaring index and scale height mainly contribute to the scatter in dust temperature at a given stellar luminosity.

For consistency, we calculated the dust masses of the Cha I disks using the same opacity, dust-temperature prescription, and updated distances. The dust masses of the USco disks are systematically lower than those of the younger Cha I population, consistent with previous studies ( Pascucci et al. 2016 ; Manara et al. 2023 ). The median dust mass of the USco sample is 0 .95 𝑀 ⊕, a factor of six lower than that of the Cha I disks (5 .69 𝑀 ⊕). Although the USco and Cha I samples span the same spectral-type range (K1–M5.5), their stellar-mass distributions are statistically different because the two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov test yields 𝑝 < 0.01%. To assess whether this affects our comparison, we repeated the analysis using only sources with 0 .08 𝑀 ⊙ < 𝑀 ★ < 0.13 𝑀 ⊙. We found median dust masses of 2 .83 𝑀 ⊕ for Cha I and 0 .44 𝑀 ⊕ for USco, indicating that the younger Cha I disks remain substantially more massive than USco disks. This result is consistent with the growing ALMA surveys demonstrating a systematic decline in millimeter dust masses with the age of star-forming regions (e.g., 

Miotello et al. 2023 ). Such a trend likely reflects the combined effects of grain growth into planetesimals, radial drift, and disk dispersal, which progressively reduce the reservoir of millimeter-sized particles responsible for the (sub-)millimeter continuum emission. Although the relative importance of these processes remains uncertain, the substantially lower dust masses observed in USco provide compelling evidence for significant evolution of the solid component over the ∼ 5 − 10 Myr age interval separating the two regions. A comparison between the masses of protoplanetary disks and mature exoplanetary systems has raised a potential “mass budget problem” for planet formation. Specifically, the dust masses inferred for many Class II disks appear insufficient to account for the masses of known exoplanet populations, particularly giant planet systems ( Manara et al. 2018 ; Andrews 2020 ). Although observational selection effects partially alleviate this discrepancy ( Mulders et al. 2021 ), the issue remains an active topic of debate ( Godines et al. 2026 ; Lee et al. 2026 ). Several solutions have been proposed. One possibility is that planet formation begins during the earlier Class 0/I stages, when disks are more massive than their Class II counterparts ( Tychoniec et al. 2018 , 2020 ). Alternatively, disks may be continuously replenished by material accreted from their natal environment, increasing the total mass available for planet formation over their lifetimes ( Manara et al. 2018 ; Gupta et al. 2024 ). A third possibility is that (sub-)millimeter continuum observations systematically underestimate disk masses because the inner region and midplane of the disk likely remain optically thick and the adopted dust opacities are uncertain ( Ballering & Eisner 2019 ; Xin et al. 2023 ; Liu et al. 2024 ). The orange curve in Figure 4 refers to the distribution of the exoplanet masses corrected for observa-tional selection effects and detection biases ( Mulders et al. 2021 ). The median mass of the exoplanets is 4.28 𝑀 ⊕, slightly lower than the median dust mass of the Cha I disks (5 .69 𝑀 ⊕). We note that our analysis is restricted to host stars with spectral types between K1 and M5.5 (Sect. 2). Including earlier-type stars will shift the dust mass distribution to higher values. In contrast, the median mass of the exoplanets is more than four times higher than the median dust mass of the USco disks (0 .95 𝑀 ⊕). This comparison has important implications for the mass-budget problem of planet formation. While the dust reservoirs in the young Cha I disks appear broadly consistent with the masses of mature planetary systems, the significantly lower dust masses observed in USco suggest that a large fraction of the solid material has already been removed from the observable millimeter-sized dust population by ages of ∼ 5 − 12 Myr. The missing solids may have been incorporated into larger bodies such as planetesimals and planets, lost through radial drift, or hidden in optically thick regions of the disk. Consequently, the low dust masses of the USco disks do not necessarily imply a lack of material for planet formation, but instead provide evidence that substantial processing of the primordial dust reservoir has already taken place by this evolutionary stage.

5 SUMMARY 

We derived dust masses of 136 full and transitional disks in the Upper Scorpius association using the recently expanded ALMA 0.88 mm continuum sample compiled by Carpenter et al. (2025 ). Our analysis incorporates a new calibration of the 𝑇 dust − 𝐿 ★ relation based on self-consistent radiative transfer models that account for stellar-mass-dependent disk sizes, accretion rates, and porous dust properties. The main results are summarized as follows. (1) We constructed a grid of 918 radiative transfer models spanning stellar luminosities from 10 −3

to 100 𝐿 ⊙. The resulting mass-averaged dust temperatures are well described by log 𝑇 dust =

1.506 + 0.115 log 𝐿 ★ + 0.004 (log 𝐿 ★)2. Compared with previous prescriptions, our relation pre-dicts systematically higher dust temperatures in the low-stellar-mass regime, largely due to the fact that disks around low-mass stars are observationally found to be smaller and therefore warmer. (2) Using the revised dust temperatures together with porous dust opacities ( 𝜅 0.88mm = 0.84 cm 2 g−1), we derived dust masses for the USco sample. The median dust mass of the USco disks (0 .95 𝑀 ⊕)is approximately six times lower than that of a comparison sample of young Cha I disks (5 .69 𝑀 ⊕). This result provides further evidence that the reservoir of millimeter-sized dust grains declines substantially over the first few Myr of disk evolution. (3) The median dust mass of the USco disks is also substantially lower than the median mass of mature exoplanetary systems. This comparison suggests that by ages of ∼ 5−12 Myr, a large fraction of the primordial solid reservoir has either been incorporated into larger bodies, removed through radial drift, or become hidden from millimeter observations. (4) We confirm a highly significant correlation between dust mass and stellar mass in both USco and Cha I. The best-fit relations are log 𝑀 dust = 1.28 + 1.65 log 𝑀 ★ for USco and log 𝑀 dust = 1.52 + 1.38 log 𝑀 ★ for Cha I. The slightly steeper relation in the older USco popula-tion is consistent with previous observational studies and with dust evolution models in which millimeter-sized grains are depleted more efficiently around low-mass stars.
