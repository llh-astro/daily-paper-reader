Abstract 

Observing auroral radio emission is one of the most promising methods for de-tecting exoplanetary magnetic fields, which provide valuable insights into planetary interiors, atmospheric properties, and potential habitability. The first hints of ex-oplanet auroral emission are starting to emerge. Recently, Zhang et al. (2025) reported a detection at 50 MHz of a circularly polarized bursty emission from the HD 189733 exoplanetary system using NenuFAR low-frequency imaging observa-tions. The source of the emission is still unknown and may be caused by planetary auroral emissions, star-planet interactions, stellar activity, or the M-dwarf stellar companion. Using the BOREALIS data reduction pipeline, we tentatively detect circularly polarized bursty emission ( ∼10 σ) from HD 189733 ∼1 hour before the burst found from the imaging observations. However, some uncertainty remains on whether our detected signal is astrophysical in nature due to excess correlated noise. Assuming an astrophysical origin, our observed characteristics are most con-sistent with a planetary origin, but stellar emission cannot be completely ruled. Therefore, more low-frequency radio observations are needed to confirm the astro-physical nature of our signal and to search for periodicity in the radio signal from HD 189733 to determine the true cause of the emission. These observations are ongoing. Our study highlights the power of simultaneous beamformed and imaging observations in the search for radio emission from exoplanets.

## 1 Introduction 

The search for auroral radio emissions from exoplanets has been ongoing for many decades (see reviews by Zarka et al. 2015; Grießmeier 2015; Grießmeier 2017; Callingham et al. 2024;Brain et al. 2024). In analogy with the magnetized Solar System planets and moons, these radio emissions are expected to be produced via the cyclotron maser instability (CMI) mechanism (Wu & Lee 1979; Zarka 2007; Treumann 2006) and be highly circu-larly polarized, beamed, and time-variable (e.g., Zarka 1998; Zarka et al. 2004; Ashtari et al. 2022). For decades, auroral radio observations has been recognized as a promising technique to probe the magnetic fields of exoplanets (e.g. Zarka et al. 1997; Farrell et al. 1999; Grießmeier 2015; Brain et al. 2024). Measuring the magnetic field of an exoplanet will give valuable constraints on its interior structure, atmospheric escape and dynamics, star-planet interactions, and potential habitability (Lazio et al., 2016; Griessmeier, 2018; Zarka, 2018; Lazio et al., 2019; Brain et al., 2024). Since the 1970’s, many ground-based observations designed to search for exoplanet radio emissions have resulted in unambiguous non-detections (e.g. Yantis et al. 1977; Winglee et al. 1986; Farrell et al. 1999; Zarka et al. 1997; Bastian et al. 2000; Lazio & Farrell 2007; Hallinan et al. 2013; Sirothia et al. 2014; Vasylieva 2015; Lynch et al. 2018; Cendes et al. 2022; Narang et al. 2023). The reviews by Grießmeier 2017 and Callingham et al. 2024 discuss these campaigns and the many degenerate reasons for the non-detections. However, the prospect of a clear detection is starting to change. Recently, circularly polarized radio emission has been tentatively detected in the τ Bo¨ otis ( τ Boo) system with LOFAR beamformed observations (Turner et al., 2021, hereafter T21), in the HD 189733 system (Zhang et al., 2025, hereafter Z25), and in several other systems using 

LOFAR imaging observations (Tasse et al., 2026). All of these tentatively detected signals are being actively followed up (e.g., Turner et al. 2023, 2024; Cordun et al. 2025). Most relevant to our study, Z25 detected circularly polarized bursts from the HD 189733 exoplanetary system using NenuFAR (New Extension in Nan¸ cay Upgrading LOFAR ;Zarka et al. 2020) imaging observations. The observations of HD 189733 were taken Tentative detection of bursty radio emission from HD 189733 using beamformed observations 3between 15.8–62.5 MHz for 103 hours, which covered most of HD 189733 b’s orbital phase. Many of these NenuFAR observations had simultaneous beamformed observations but Z25 only analyzed the imaging observations. In their analysis, Z25 generated dy-namic spectra for the target and other sources in the field using residual visibilities with the new RIMS technique (Tasse et al., 2026), followed by a transient search in the time-frequency plane. Z25 detected (6 σ) a highly circularly polarized radio burst on 2023-09-28 at 21:18 UTC for 96 seconds (centered around the transit, phase of 0, of HD 189733 b) spanning the frequency range 47.6–52.1 MHz with a flux density of 1.5 Jy. Z25 narrowed the cause of the emission to four possible scenarios. Two of the scenarios are caused by star-planet interactions (SPI; Callingham et al. 2024): (1) sub-Alfv´ enic SPI (analogous to the Jupiter-Io system; Goldreich & Lynden-Bell 1969) and (2) wind-magnetosphere planetary auroral emission (Zarka, 1998). The last two scenarios are caused by stellar activity (Dulk, 1985; Vedantham et al., 2020), either by the primary K-star or M-dwarf companion: (3) Stellar CMI emission and (4) stellar plasma emission from flares. Finally, Z25 encouraged follow-up observations to search for periodicity in the signal to determine its origin and confirm their result. Motivated by the detection of bursty radio emission from HD 189733 (Z25), we analyzed the beamformed data from NenuFAR that was taken simultaneously to the imaging data that found the bursty signal. This dataset enables an independent confirmation of the detected burst through an alternative backend and processing workflow. 

## 2 Observations 

For this study, we analyzed the NenuFAR beamformed observations of HD 189733 taken simultaneously with the imaging observations on 2023-09-28 from 19:31:12–22:47:33 UTC (19.52–22.79 UTC). An example dynamic spectrum of this observation can be found in Figure 1. This data is part of a large multi-year NenuFAR campaign on HD 189733 and the results of this larger campaign will be presented elsewhere. The beamformed observations were obtained with the UnDySPuTeD receiver (Bondonneau et al., 2021), which provides the full Stokes parameters. The setup of the observations can be found in Table 1 and the positions of the ON- and OFF-beams can be found in Table 2. The location of the OFF-beams were chosen such that they were devoid of point radio sources (flux ≥ 300 mJy) from the LOFAR Multifrequency Snapshot Sky Survey ( MSSS ; Heald et al. 2015). Here, we will only concentrate on the Stokes V (circularly polarized flux) data since auroral radio emissions are expected to be circularly polarized (Zarka 1998), Turner et al. (2019, hereafter T19) showed that Stokes V is an order of magnitude more sensitive than Stokes I for detecting attenuated Jovian radio bursts, and the Z25 detection was in Stokes V. The observations consist of an on-target beam (“ON-beam”) and three beams pointing to a nearby location in the sky (“OFF-beam 1”, “OFF-beam 2”, and “OFF-beam 3”). As in our previous studies (Turner et al., 2017, 2019, 2021, 2023, 2024), we use the OFF-beams to characterize any terrestrial ionospheric fluctuations, RFI (radio frequency interference), and instrumental systematics in the data. In the final analysis, the OFF-beams are used to remove all these signals from the ON-beam. Therefore, any remaining 4 Turner, Zarka, Grie ßmeier, Louis, et al.

## 3 Analysis 

The beamformed data was pre-processed following the standard procedure as described in Turner et al. (2023) and briefly described below. The raw (level 1 data; L1) beamformed observations from NenuFAR are produced by coherently summing all the signals from each dipole antenna. The reduction pipeline computes the 4 Stokes parameters (I, Q, U, and V), applies RFI mitigation, corrects for the time variations of the instrumental gain, and finally bins the data on 250 ms × 48 kHz bins. This data is labeled as level 2 data (L2). The L2 data for HD 189733 taken on 2023-09-28 can be found in Figure 1A. In this data, many large scale systematics are visible. The source of several of these features have a known origin. For example, we see background baseline changes every 6 minutes due to the analog beams of NenuFAR being re-pointed for source tracking. Next, the patchy darker bands and streaks above 30 MHz are caused by bright A-team sources in 

NenuFAR ’s grating lobes. We searched the L2 data for bursty emission in Stokes V using the BOREALIS (BeamfOrmed Radio Emission AnaLysIS) pipeline (Turner et al. 2017, 2019, 2021). BOREALIS has been verified on attenuated Jupiter data from LOFAR and pulsar data from NenuFAR and 

LOFAR . We performed RFI mitigation using a similar setup as in Turner et al. (2023). After RFI mitigation we re-binned the data to a time and frequency resolution of ∆ τ

(either 0.2, 1, or 8 seconds) and 45 kHz, respectively. This rebinned data was then processed through the post-processing part of BOREALIS . A very detailed description of the burst emission observables can be found in T19 and T21 and we describe them briefly below. The post-processing is initially performed on the absolute value of the corrected Stokes V data as defined in T19. For burst emission, we use the Q2, Q3a-f, and Q4a-f observable quantities, described hereafter. The Q2 observable consists of one time series per beam (ON, OFF1, OFF2, and OFF3). To search for faint emission, the high-pass filtered dynamic spectrum is integrated over a large frequency range (e.g., bin sizes of 10 MHz) to produce the Q2 time series. Q2 can be represented by a “scatter plot” comparing a pair of beams (e.g., the ON and one of the OFF beams) and is designed to find bursty emission (with time scales < 1 minute). The Q3 and Q4 quantities (a-f) provide statistical measures of the bursts identified in the high-pass filtered time series (Q2). Specifically, Q3 searches for signals vs time with 2 minute time bins and Q4 integrates over all time. When examining Q3 and Q4, the time series of the two beams (ON vs OFF or OFF vs OFF) are compared to each other. For this, we introduce the difference curve Q nDiff =Qn(ON)-Qn(OFF), where n is equal to a-f. The observable quantities (Q3, Q4, Qn Diff ) are always compared against a reference curve computed from 10000 draws of purely Gaussian noise. The post-processing was initially performed over four different frequency ranges (21–58 6 Turner, Zarka, Grie ßmeier, Louis, et al. 

MHz, 21–40 MHz, and 40–58 MHz, 47–52 MHz) and two separate time ranges (19:31– 21:09 UT, 21:09–22:47 UT). The 47–52 MHz range represents the frequency coverage of the burst found by Z25. All other post-processing parameters are similar to those of T21. In this paper, we search for burst emission in the data for a ∆ τ of 0.1, 1, and 8 seconds and high-passed filtered with a smoothing timescale of 10∆ τ . The 8 second binned data is designed to be directly comparable to the imaging analysis (Z25). For comparison, T21 only observed burst emission at a ∆ τ of 1 second for τ Boo. For completeness, we also searched for bursts on the RIMS dynamic spectra produced from the imaging observations (Z25) with BOREALIS . This dynamic spectra is binned to a ∆ τ =8 seconds. We used nearly the same beams coordinates for the RIMS OFF-beams. All the same steps as above were performed. 

## 4 Results 

We searched for an excess signal in the ON-beam both by eye and using the automated search procedure and criteria outlined in T21 and T19. We find a strong signal of circularly polarized bursty emission in the NenuFAR beamformed observation between 21–40 MHz from 19:31–21:09 UT at a resolution of 1 second (Figure 2). Most importantly, the signal can be clearly seen in all statistical measures and the OFF beam analysis does not show any signs of false positives (several are shown in Figure 2). As expected from our previous studies (T19; T21), we find the most significant detection of 7.4 σ in the Q4f statistic. The signal looks exactly like attenuated Jupiter burst emission (see Figure 3 in the Appendix and Fig. C.2 from T21). We do not detect any emission in any other parameter permutation or in the RIMS dynamic spectra. Next, we performed a more fine-tuned search on the detection in time, frequency, and the sign of the polarization. The signal is found to be most pronounced in the V − analysis, suggesting that the emission is left-hand polarized. Also, we narrowed the frequency range of the detection to be between 27–40 MHz. Using the time-series of the bursty emission (Q3), we find that the burst ( >2σ) occurred between 19:40–19:56 UT (19.67– 19.93 UT) for a total duration of 16 minutes (Figure 2E–F). Again, the Q3 detection looks like strong attenuated Jupiter burst emission (see Fig. 7 in T19). These bursts become dilated at longer time resolutions (8 seconds), so they shouldn’t be visible in the Z25 imaging observations. The signal rises to a 10 σ detection using Q3 when compared to random Gaussian noise. In Q3, the OFF-beams only show a few spurious signals up to 2 σ greatly strengthening the reliability of the detection. We can find an estimate of the flux of the detected signal using the high-pass filtered time-series (Q2; Figure 2A). The maximum flux value detected in Q2 was 7.6 σbg . The main concern to estimate σbg is whether the observation is white-noise dominated. Therefore, we performed a red-noise analysis on the Q2 time series using the wavelet technique of Carter & Winn (2009) as implemented in the EXOMOP code by Turner et al. (2016). The wavelet method models the time series as the sum of Gaussian white noise and red noise, with the red noise component having a power spectral density proportional to 1 /f α. ## 5 Discussion and conclusions 

From our analysis, we find tentative signs of circularly polarized bursty emission from HD 189733 detected in the NenuFAR beamformed observations. A summary of the burst characteristics in our beamformed observations and the imaging observations (Z25) can be found in Table 3. Our observed properties are mainly different than those found for the imaging observations. However, this is not entirely unexpected regardless of the cause of Tentative detection of bursty radio emission from HD 189733 using beamformed observations 9the emission. The radio emissions from the Solar System planets (Zarka, 1998), the Sun (Dulk, 1985), and other stars (Dulk, 1985; Driessen et al., 2024) are extremely variable over many orders of magnitudes in time and frequency with changing polarization. Our result is most consistent with CMI emission (Zarka, 2007). If the signal is confirmed to be astrophysical of nature, our characteristics can be modeled with ExPRES (Hess & Zarka, 2011; Louis et al., 2019; Chebly et al., 2025) to provide more constraints on the origin of the bursty emission, which we leave for future work. Assuming an astrophysical signal, this observation adds more evidence alongside the detec-tion by Z25 to the possibility of radio signals originating from the HD 189733 system. The four possible sources of the emission are: (1) sub-Alfv´ enic SPI, (2) wind-magnetosphere interaction, (3) stellar CMI, and (4) stellar plasma emission. Each of these cases were discussed in length in (Z25). All of our observed characteristics can support one and/or more of these scenarios (see Table 3). Each of these scenarios are discussed briefly below: 

• (1) Sub-Alfv´ enic SPI and (3) Stellar CMI: While several of our observed properties are consistent with these scenarios, our observed frequency range supports them even less than Z25. We find a plasma-to-cyclotron frequency ratio (f pe / f ce ) between 2.1-2.3 for our observed frequency range assuming a 40 G dipolar magnetic field (Strugarek et al., 2022) and a nominal coronal base density of 10 8 cm 3 (Withbroe, 1988). This value is higher than that found in (Z25) and much higher than the 0.1 nominal value needed to suppresses the CMI mechanism (Grießmeier et al., 2007; Weber et al., 2017). A localized region with an enhanced magnetic field strength (between 150–200 G) on the stellar surface is the only resolution for this problem. 

• (2) Wind-magnetosphere interaction: Among the scenarios considered, the wind-magnetosphere interaction is the most consistent with the observed properties, if the signal is astrophysical. Remarkably, our individual burst durations ( ∼1 sec) resembles that of S-burst emissions from Jupiter (Riihimaa, 1977; Carr et al., 1983). Similarly, such short duration bursts were tentatively detected on the exoplanetary system τ boo using LOFAR beamformed observations (T21). Radiative transfer modeling done by Kavanagh et al. (2019) found that the planetary radio emission from HD 189733 b may only escape the system when the planet is near primary transit. On the other hand, as only one orbit of HD 189733 b was observed, the orbital phase alignment may be quintessential. 

• (4) Stellar plasma emission: Most of the of the observed characteristics support this scenario with the exception of the brightness temperature. Specifically, our individual burst durations ( ∼1 sec) resemble that of Type I bursts from the sun (Wild et al., 1963). The plasma emission mechanism proposed for Type I solar radio emission should not exceed a TB ∼ 3 × 10 9 K (Melrose, 1980). Similarly, plasma emission under typical coronal conditions (for all types of stellar emission) saturates at T B ∼ 10 12 K (Dulk, 1985). Thus, this mechanism is not preferred (unless we are under very extreme circumstances such as a flare) since our observed TB is many orders of magnitude higher than these limits. Therefore, our detection is most consistent, similar to that found by Z25, with plane-tary auroral emission from wind-magnetosphere interactions with a planetary magnetic field constraint between ∼12–18 G but stellar emission cannot be completely ruled out 10 Turner, Zarka, Grie ßmeier, Louis, et al. 

especially under extreme stellar circumstances (e.g, flares). Although the signal we find is statistically significant when compared to Gaussian noise, there are several aspects to our data that call into question a conclusive detection. We find evidence of excess correlated noise (e.g., low-level RFI or instrumental systematics) left in the data. First, this noise can clearly be seen as the diagonal cloud of points in Figure 2A–B. An observation without correlated noise should appear as a near perfect sphere with most of the points below 3 σ. Likewise, the non-spherical shape of the main cloud also indicates non-Gaussian noise. As a reminder, the Q4e–Q4f values are specifically designed to ignore the correlated diagonal data in their calculation (see Fig. 5 in T19). Therefore, this correlated noise is not included in our detection calculations. Second, there is excess signal between η=1–2 sigma in the OFF-beams that does not behave like random noise. Similarly, our detection criteria (as outlined in T21) only consider η values above 1.5 sigma. For random noise, the curves should be mainly concentrated between the 1 σ Gaussian simulations. These two problems seem unique to NenuFAR as they were not found in our LOFAR analysis using BOREALIS of 100’s of hours of data from three exoplanetary systems (T21) and the attenuated Jupiter data (T19). An example of an observation without correlated noise in any of the diagnostic plots can be found in Figure 3 in the Appendix. Due to these concerns, we classify our finding as a tentative detection of an astrophysical signal. Therefore, more observations are needed to confirm the astrophysical nature and to search for periodicity in our detected signal.
